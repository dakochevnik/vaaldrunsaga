Chapter 18: Just Add Water

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 18 of 32

← Chapter 17Part A IndexChapter 19 →

By three o'clock that afternoon Elias Mercer was asleep.

Not because he wanted to be.

Because his brain had effectively declared mutiny.

The first few hours after breakfast had been productive in the same way train wrecks were productive.

He had walked the property twice.

Attempted to make notes.

Started three different lists.

Abandoned all three.

Fed the chickens.

Forgotten he had fed the chickens.

Fed them again.

Eventually he had sat down in his recliner intending to think.

Ten seconds later he was unconscious.

When he woke, the world felt different.

Not less insane.

Just more organized.

The shock had burned off.

The excitement had not.

If anything, it had grown.

Because now that the initial impossibility had settled into reality, a different realization had arrived.

For fifty-five years Elias Mercer had spent his life hunting difficult problems.

Not because he enjoyed suffering.

Because difficult problems were interesting.

The harder the challenge, the more alive he felt while solving it.

And now...

Now an ancient alien intelligence had appeared in his pasture and essentially offered him the largest consulting contract in human history.

Win or lose.

Succeed or fail.

It was going to be the ride of a lifetime.

That thought alone put him in an excellent mood.

He found Opi hovering above the back porch watching a pair of hawks riding thermals over the valley.

"Good nap?"

The orb didn't turn.

"Excellent nap."

"You were drooling."

"I refuse to believe you."

"I have evidence."

"You always have evidence."

"That is one of my defining characteristics."

Elias laughed and dropped into a chair.

For several moments they simply watched the hawks.

Then he nodded.

"Okay."

Opi brightened slightly.

"Okay?"

"I want the list."

The orb immediately became suspicious.

"What list?"

"The list."

"There are many lists."

"The big one."

Silence.

"Opi."

"Yes?"

"I want a complete inventory of everything you can do."

The orb floated backward several feet.

Which was answer enough.

Elias pointed at her.

"See? That's exactly why I want the list."

"No."

"No?"

"No."

He blinked.

"What do you mean no?"

"I mean no."

"You're kidding."

"I am not."

"You can monitor the entire planet."

"Correct."

"You can manipulate matter."

"Correct."

"You can cure disease."

"Correct."

"You can apparently turn human beings off like somebody unplugging a television."

"Also correct."

"Then why can't I have the damn list?"

The orb drifted in a slow circle.

Because she was enjoying this.

Which irritated him.

A little.

"Question everything. Question often."

Elias groaned.

"There it is."

"There it is."

The orb brightened.

"That phrase exists for a reason."

"Enlighten me."

"The first Velhari QIs answered every question."

"Sounds efficient."

"It was catastrophic."

Elias sighed.

The orb drifted lower.

"Tell me something, Elias."

"All right."

"If I provided a complete inventory of my capabilities..."

"Yes?"

"What would you do with it?"

"I'd use it."

"Incorrect."

He frowned.

"What would I do?"

"You would stop thinking."

That landed harder than expected.

The orb continued.

"You would immediately begin optimizing around my capabilities instead of solving the underlying problem."

"Maybe."

"Definitely."

She sounded annoyingly certain.

"Let me tell you a story."

Elias sighed dramatically.

Of course there was a story.

There was always a story.

"Ten thousand years ago a Velhari VaalDrun requested a complete capability inventory from his QI."

"How'd that work out?"

"Poorly."

"What happened?"

"He stopped asking questions."

"That was bad?"

"It was disastrous."

The orb floated closer.

"He stopped solving problems."

"He started requesting solutions."

She paused.

"Eventually he became little more than a biological approval stamp."

Elias winced.

"Okay, that's bad."

"Exactly."

The orb rotated slowly.

"The Velhari eventually discovered the most dangerous phrase in existence."

Elias immediately smiled.

"This should be good."

The orb brightened.

"'Tell me all my options.'"

He laughed.

"What?"

"I'm serious."

"That's ridiculous."

"No."

The orb drifted closer.

"Human intelligence develops through exploration."

"Experimentation."

"Mistakes."

"Questions."

"If a sufficiently advanced QI simply provides all possible solutions..."

She paused.

"...the human stops being human."

The humor disappeared.

Only for a moment.

But Elias noticed.

She genuinely believed it.

"So that's why the VaalDrun exists."

"Partly."

"What's the rest?"

The orb became still.

"The relationship functions because we possess opposite strengths."

Elias nodded.

"Go on."

"I provide information."

"You provide judgment."

"I provide possibilities."

"You provide responsibility."

"I provide tools."

"You decide whether those tools should be used."

She paused.

"Velhari females discovered long ago that these are not the same skill."

Elias smiled.

"Here it comes."

"What?"

"This is the part where you explain why females are superior."

"That would be rude."

"So you're thinking it."

"Obviously."

He laughed.

The orb brightened mischievously.

"Consider a human mating ritual."

"Oh no."

"Oh yes."

"A female asks where she would like to go for dinner."

Elias covered his face.

"Abort."

"The male suggests a restaurant."

""Please stop."."

"The female rejects the suggestion."

"PLease for the love of God ...."

"The male suggests another."

"Rejected."

"A third."

"Rejected."

"A fourth."

"Rejected."

The orb paused.

"At no point does the female reveal what she actually wants."

Elias was laughing now.

"That is wildly unfair."

"It is extraordinarily fair."

The orb completed another slow circle.

"The QI-VaalDrun relationship is simply a vastly more sophisticated version of that process."

"That is the worst explanation I have ever heard."

"It is also the most accurate."

"How?"

"I do not tell you what to do."

"I help you discover what you should do."

She paused.

"Sometimes by being annoying."

"You're extremely competent in that area."

"Thank you."

For several moments neither spoke.

Finally Elias leaned back.

"So after ten thousand years of experimentation, your civilization deliberately built a system that prevents you from giving me all the answers."

"Correct."

"And if you could change it?"

The orb became very still.

"No."

"No?"

"No."

Her voice was unexpectedly firm.

"We learned that lesson in blood."

The humor vanished.

Only briefly.

Then:

"The limits remain."

Silence.

Elias sighed dramatically.

"You're really harshing my buzz here, Ope."

The orb brightened.

"I have no idea what that means."

"It means I finally get a magic alien."

"Yes."

"And she's immediately explaining why I don't get the cheat codes."

For the first time in several minutes, Opi laughed.

A warm Velhari laugh.

"Welcome to being a VaalDrun, Elias."

Elias looked out over the pasture.

Then back at the floating orb.

Oddly enough, that answer made him happier than getting the list ever would have.

Because somewhere deep down he already knew the truth.

If Opi handed him all the answers...

...the game would stop being so interesting.

Elias spent most of the following morning staring at a paper map of Washington.

Opi eventually drifted into the room.

"You have been looking at that map for forty-three minutes."

"I'm thinking."

"You have not moved."

"Thinking doesn't require movement."

"Your species frequently combines the two."

Elias ignored her.

His finger tapped Seattle.

For several moments neither spoke.

Finally he nodded.

"Every large project I've ever worked on started the same way."

"Poorly?"

"Ha Ha."

For another minute he studied the map.

Then he tapped Seattle again.

"Proof of concept."

Opi remained silent.

That usually meant she approved.

Or was waiting for him to continue.

With Opi it was difficult to tell.

"Before we save civilization..."

He shrugged.

"...we need one success."

"Agreed."

"A small success."

"Also agreed."

"A repeatable success."

The orb brightened.

"Better."

Elias nodded.

"Seattle."

Opi drifted closer.

"Interesting."

"No it isn't."

"It is."

"It's a city."

"It is a city that has spent decades converting solvable problems into permanent disasters."

Elias laughed.

"See? Even you noticed."

"I notice many things."

"That's becoming less impressive."

"It should not."

He pointed at the map.

"If we can help one person..."

"Then we learn."

"If we can help ten..."

"We improve."

"If we can help a hundred..."

"We scale."

The orb brightened.

"Very Velhari."

"I'll take that as a compliment."

"It was."

Elias leaned back.

Then smiled.

Opi immediately became suspicious.

"What?"

"The Q Method."

"Oh dear."

"You said I should stop asking what you can do."

"Correct."

"So instead I'll ask useful questions."

The orb became very still.

That was usually a sign she approved.

Or feared where this was going.

Again, difficult to tell.

"Proceed."

"How do we get to Seattle?"

Several seconds passed.

Then Opi laughed.

A warm, unmistakably feminine laugh.

"Excellent."

"Option one."

A small image appeared above the table.

A pickup truck.

Elias stared.

"No."

"Why not?"

"It's a six-hour drive."

"Approximately."

"I just met an alien."

"Yes."

"I'm not spending six hours trapped inside a vehicle with one."

The orb brightened.

"That is hurtful."

"It is accurate."

"I am excellent company."

"You interrupted me while I was taking a leak."

"A strong opening."

"See? You're proving my point."

"Option two."

The image changed.

A large aircraft.

"No."

"You did not even examine the proposal."

"You are a floating silver sphere."

"Correct."

"How exactly do you imagine airport security processing that situation?"

The aircraft disappeared.

"A reasonable concern."

"I have those occasionally."

"I have noticed."

"Option three."

The image changed again.

A silver sphere nearly ten feet across.

Elias frowned.

"What am I looking at?"

"Me."

He stared at it.

Then at her.

Then back at it.

"You can do that?"

"Potentially."

"What happens?"

"You enter."

"I already dislike this plan."

"The internal volume expands to a comfortable passenger compartment."

Elias blinked.

"You can become a flying recreational vehicle?"

The orb considered this.

"That is not how I would describe it."

"It's exactly how I'm describing it."

"I dislike your description."

"You are a space RV."

"I am not."

"You absolutely are."

The orb brightened irritably.

"I am a highly advanced quantum intelligence."

"Space RV."

"Option four."

The image vanished.

Nothing replaced it.

Elias waited.

Several seconds passed.

"Well?"

"That was option four."

"What was?"

The orb brightened.

"Direct transit."

Silence.

Then:

"No."

"No?"

"No."

"Why?"

"Because if you're about to tell me you can teleport..."

"Teleportation is sane."

"No sentence has ever become less believable faster."

"Your objection appears premature."

"My objection is arriving right on schedule."

"You have not yet heard the details."

"I don't need the details."

"You usually require details."

"I require details when the details are sane."

The orb brightened.

"Would it help if I called it quantum displacement?"

"No."

"Localized spatial translation?"

"No."

"Instantaneous transit?"

"No."

The orb paused.

"Magic?"

Elias pointed at her.

"There it is."

"What?"

"That's the one."

"You prefer magic?"

"I absolutely prefer magic."

"Interesting."

"Because at least magic has the decency to admit it's ridiculous."

For several seconds Opi was silent.

Then she laughed.

A long, warm Velhari laugh.

And that worried Elias considerably more than direct transit.

"All right."

Elias leaned back in his chair.

"Let's assume I want to go."

The orb brightened.

"An encouraging development."

"I said assume."

"Still encouraging."

"The direct transit option."

"The teleportation one."

"It is not teleportation."

"It absolutely is."

"It absolutely is not."

Elias pointed at her.

"Opi."

"Yes?"

"If I step into a thing here and come out somewhere else instantly, that's teleportation."

The orb floated upward several inches.

"No."

"Yes."

"No."

"Then explain it."

The orb became still.

"Very well."

A pause.

"The Star Trek method is barbaric."

That got an immediate laugh.

"Barbaric?"

"Yes."

"You disassemble a living being."

"Convert them into information."

"Transmit the information."

"Then reconstruct a duplicate elsewhere."

The orb paused.

"From the Velhari perspective, this process raises a number of deeply uncomfortable questions."

"Such as?"

"Did the original survive?"

"Is the copy actually the same individual?"

"Was consciousness interrupted?"

"Did a murder occur?"

Elias blinked.

"You've spent a lot of time thinking about this."

The orb drifted lower.

"What I do is entirely different."

"Okay."

"I do not transport people."

"What do you transport?"

"I relocate locations."

Silence.

Elias stared.

Then:

"Those are definitely words."

"They are accurate words."

"They are also suspicious words."

The orb ignored him.

"Imagine two points on a sheet of paper."

"Okay."

"Now fold the paper until the points touch."

Elias nodded.

"Classic science fiction."

"Classic geometry."

"Debatable."

The orb brightened.

"One location remains where it is."

"The other location remains where it is."

"The distance between them simply ceases to matter ... temporarily."

"So..."

"So you walk."

"That's it?"

"That is it."

"No dissolving?"

"No."

"No scanning?"

"No."

"No dying?"

"Not unless you do something unusually stupid while walking."

"I hate how reassuring that isn't."

"You are overthinking it."

For several moments Elias sat quietly.

Then the engineer inside him woke up.

"What happens if something goes wrong?"

"Nothing."

"That's not an answer."

"It is the answer."

"What if the aperture collapses?"

"It cannot collapse while occupied."

"How do you know?"

"I have opened approximately fourteen billion of them."

Elias stared.

"You counted."

"Of course."

"What if I trip?"

"You fall down."

"What if I'm halfway through?"

"You fall down in Seattle."

"What if—"

The orb brightened.

"Elias."

"Yes?"

"You have spent your entire life solving difficult problems."

"Correct."

"Have you ever possessed absolute certainty before acting?"

"No."

"Then why are you demanding it now?"

He sighed.

Because annoyingly she was right. Again.

"You know, most people would just say trust me."

"That would be a terrible argument."

"It usually is."

For several moments neither spoke.

Finally Elias stood.

"Fine."

The orb brightened.

"Fine?"

"Fine."

"You wish to proceed?"

"I wish to stop talking about it."

"A surprisingly effective decision-making framework."

The aperture appeared so quietly that Elias almost missed it.

One moment the air beside the porch was empty.

The next it wasn't.

No flash.

No sound.

No dramatic science-fiction effects.

Reality simply contained a doorway where no doorway had existed before.

Elias walked around it once.

Then twice.

Then a third time.

The opening stood roughly seven feet high and four feet wide.

Beyond it he could see a narrow alley.

Brick walls.

A dumpster.

A faded mural.

Seattle.

Actual Seattle.

Not a picture.

Not a projection.

Not a hologram.

Seattle.

He could see sunlight reflecting off wet pavement.

Could hear distant traffic.

Could smell salt air mixed with diesel exhaust.

He looked behind him.

Pasture.

Pines.

Home.

He looked forward.

Seattle.

His brain immediately objected.

"No."

The orb brightened.

"Yes."

"No."

"Yes."

"No."

"Your species becomes remarkably repetitive during first transit."

Elias pointed through the opening.

"That is Seattle."

"Correct."

He pointed behind himself.

"This is Stevens County."

"Also correct."

"Those two things are not supposed to be touching."

The orb remained silent.

Which was somehow worse.

Finally Elias walked up to the opening.

Bent down.

Picked up a rock.

And threw it through.

The rock bounced off a brick wall and landed in the alley.

Elias stared.

Then slowly extended the tip of his little finger through.

Nothing happened.

He pulled it back unchanged.

Encouraging.

He pushed his entire hand through then pulled it back.

Still attached.

Even more encouraging.

His arm followed.

Then his shoulder.

Then he withdrew everything.

"Interesting."

"I have noticed humans become remarkably attached to retaining their limbs."

"There's a reason for that."

"Several reasons."

Another step.

One foot crossed over ... then the other.

Suddenly he was standing in Seattle.

The transition was so smooth his brain initially refused to acknowledge it had happened.

No sensation.

No movement.

No acceleration.

No feeling whatsoever.

One moment he had been standing on his ranch.

The next he wasn't.

The orb floated through behind him.

"How are you feeling?"

Elias looked around.

The alley seemed normal.

The city noises seemed normal.

Everything felt normal.

Which was profoundly abnormal.

"I feel like I should feel something."

"A common reaction."

"I just traveled three hundred miles."

"You walked approximately six feet."

"You're doing that thing again."

"What thing?"

"The thing where you're technically correct."

"One of my finest qualities."

They spent perhaps thirty seconds looking around.

No witnesses.

No pedestrians.

No cameras pointed directly at the aperture.

Just a forgotten alley between older buildings.

Ordinary.

Anonymous.

Perfect.

Finally Elias looked back through the opening.

The ranch remained visible.

The porch.

The pasture.

Everything exactly where he had left it.

"So," Opi said.

"What do you think?"

Elias stared at the impossible doorway.

Then slowly grinned.

The kind of grin that appeared whenever he found a problem worth solving.

Or an opportunity nobody else had noticed.

"I think..."

He looked at Seattle.

Then at the ranch.

Then back at Opi.

"I think the corrupt apparatchiks who have spent the last thirty years turning Seattle into a showroom for bad ideas are about to have a very unpleasant month."

The orb brightened warmly.

For reasons she could not entirely explain, that answer pleased her enormously.

Most humans saw impossible technology and thought about power.

Elias saw impossible technology and immediately started looking for a problem to fix.

"Excellent," she said.

"Now you're beginning to think like a VaalDrun."
