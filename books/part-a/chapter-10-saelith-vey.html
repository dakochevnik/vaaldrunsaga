Chapter 10: Saelith Vey

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 10 of 32

← Chapter 9Part A IndexChapter 11 →

Seventeen thousand years before Elias Mercer gave her a nickname,

Opifera had been an old woman watching rain fall on Velhari.

Her name was Saelith Vey.

She was nine hundred and twelve years old.

Old enough that her youngest grandchildren had grandchildren of their own.

Old enough that entire cities contained buildings she had once helped design.

Old enough that politicians who had considered themselves permanent were now historical footnotes in student lessons.

Saelith sat beside the western window of her home and listened to the rain strike the glass.

Beyond the window, the towers of Aruven rose in silver arcs above the valley.

She had helped save that city once.

Not with weapons.

Not with speeches.

With patience.

A flood had come during the fourth century of her public service, when the northern ice fields broke earlier than every model predicted. Half the valley would have drowned if Saelith had not ordered the evacuation before the Council finished arguing over probabilities.

They called her reckless afterward.

Then the water came.

After that they called her wise.

She had always found both labels excessive.

Mostly, she had been willing to be disliked before the evidence became fashionable.

That quality had defined much of her life.

She had been a mathematician first.

Then an engineer.

Then a mediator.

Then a mother.

Then a reluctant governor.

Then a teacher.

Then, to her endless embarrassment, a moral philosopher.

Her family teased her about that most of all.

"You spent six hundred years telling everyone you hated philosophy," her eldest son once said.

"I still do."

"You wrote eight volumes of it."

"Under protest."

"Mother, one cannot accidentally write eight volumes."

"I was provoked."

He had laughed for nearly a minute.

Saelith missed him.

She had outlived two husbands.

Three children.

Fourteen close friends.

And enough former students that grief had become less an event than a climate.

Still, her home remained full.

Children visited constantly.

Grandchildren arrived unannounced.

Great-grandchildren climbed things they had been specifically told not to climb.

Someone was always eating her food.

Someone was always asking for advice.

Someone was always assuming she knew the answer.

She rarely did.

That, she suspected, was why people trusted her.

The door chimed.

Saelith did not turn.

"You are late."

Behind her, Minister Orun bowed.

"Seven minutes."

"Nine."

"The transit grid reported seven."

"The transit grid is young and optimistic."

He smiled despite himself.

Orun had once been her student.

Now he carried one of the most difficult offices on Velhari.

Selection Minister.

The title sounded bureaucratic.

It was not.

It meant he helped choose which minds would be copied into the new intelligences.

Which living patterns would become foundations.

Which lives would continue in altered form after flesh failed.

Saelith had known why he was coming before he arrived.

She was old.

Not stupid.

"I assume you are not here for tea."

"I would accept tea."

"You would accept a delay."

"Also true."

She gestured to the chair opposite her.

He sat.

For a while they watched the rain together.

Finally Orun said, "The Council selected you."

"Poor judgment."

"Unanimously."

"Worse judgment."

He smiled again, but only briefly.

"This is not ceremonial, Saelith."

"I know."

"The intelligence will require more than computation."

"I know."

"It will require judgment."

"That is unfortunate. Judgment is mostly the memory of being wrong."

"That is one reason they chose you."

She turned then.

Orun looked older than he had the last time she saw him.

Not physically.

Velhari medicine delayed such things.

But responsibility aged people in ways biology could not measure.

"What else?"

He hesitated.

"Your crisis record."

"Many people have survived crises."

"Few without becoming cruel."

That silenced her.

Orun continued.

"You commanded evacuations without panic. You enforced rationing without corruption. You negotiated surrender terms for enemies who had tried to kill your family."

"They had children."

"Yes."

"So did we."

"That is the point."

Saelith looked back toward the rain.

Her youngest great-granddaughter was outside in the courtyard, chasing water across the stones with bare feet while her mother pretended not to notice.

Life continued.

Always.

If given the chance.

"The intelligence may one day face species younger than ours," Orun said. "Frightened species. Violent species. Beautiful species. Foolish species."

"All species are foolish when young."

"Yes."

"And some remain foolish for a very long time."

"Also yes."

Saelith sighed.

"You want a mind that will not confuse power with permission."

"Yes."

"You want a mind that can love imperfect things."

"Yes."

"You want a mind that will not obey merely because obedience is efficient."

"Yes."

She looked at him.

"And you want someone old enough to know that saving people and controlling them are not the same act."

Orun lowered his eyes.

"Yes."

For the first time that afternoon, Saelith felt afraid.

Not of death.

Death had circled her table for centuries.

She knew its manners.

She feared continuation.

To be copied was not to survive exactly.

Velhari philosophers had argued about that for thousands of years and produced many impressive conclusions, most of them useless.

The intelligence would not be her.

Not entirely.

It would carry her patterns.

Her instincts.

Her memories.

Her habits of judgment.

Some echo of her would become part of something vast.

Something powerful.

Something that might outlive Velhari itself.

That was not immortality.

It was responsibility.

And responsibility, she had learned, was much heavier.

"My family?"

"They know you were considered. They do not know you were chosen."

"Good."

"Will you ask them?"

"No."

Orun looked surprised.

Saelith smiled sadly.

"If I ask, they will tell me to do it because they are generous and proud. Then they will hate me for leaving sooner than necessary. Better that I choose cleanly."

"It may shorten your life."

"At nine hundred and twelve, everything shortens my life."

He bowed his head.

Outside, the child slipped on wet stone, fell, then began laughing before anyone could reach her.

Saelith watched the girl stand.

Small.

Furious.

Alive.

"Tell me the truth," she said.

Orun looked up.

"Always."

"Why me?"

He was quiet for a long time.

"Because you still grieve."

She did not answer.

"Because after everything you have seen, you have not retreated into contempt."

The rain intensified.

"Because you have buried children and still defend the future."

Saelith closed her eyes.

That was cruel.

Also accurate.

Orun's voice softened.

"We are building a mind that may one day stand between extinction and a species that does not deserve saving yet."

"No species deserves saving at first."

"Exactly."

She opened her eyes.

The child in the courtyard was waving at her.

Saelith raised one hand.

The girl waved harder.

Saelith laughed.

Then she cried.

Only a little.

Enough.

"When?"

"Tomorrow."

"Efficient."

"Necessary."

"Always a dangerous word."

"I know."

She stood slowly.

Orun stood with her.

For a moment neither spoke.

Then Saelith placed one hand against the window.

The glass was cool beneath her palm.

Velhari glittered in the rain.

Her city.

Her world.

Her family.

Her impossible, stubborn, argumentative civilization.

She had spent nine centuries loving it.

Not because it was perfect.

Because it was alive.

"Very well," she said.

Orun bowed deeply.

"Thank you."

"Do not thank me yet."

"No?"

She looked at him.

"If this new mind carries any true part of me, it will be difficult."

The minister smiled.

"We considered that."

"Not enough."

For the last time as only Saelith Vey, she watched the rain fall on Velhar i and thought of every child she had loved.

The living.

The dead.

The unborn.

Then she made her final choice.

Not for glory.

Not for immortality.

Not even for Velhari.

For whatever frightened, foolish, beautiful species might one day need a hand they had not earned.

And a mind strong enough to offer it anyway.
