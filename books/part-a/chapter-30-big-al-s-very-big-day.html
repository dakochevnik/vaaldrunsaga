Chapter 30: Big Al's Very Big Day

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 30 of 32

← Chapter 29Part A IndexChapter 31 →

The text arrived shortly after breakfast.

Elias:

On a special trip. I'll be in your neighborhood around six. Need to talk with you privately.

Big Al frowned.

That was unusual.

Not the visit.

The privately part.

The Mercer family generally treated privacy as a manufacturing defect.

Still, it was Elias.

If his cousin wanted to talk, there was probably a reason.

Big Al:

Sure. Everything okay?

Several minutes passed.

Finally:

Elias:

Yep.

A second message appeared.

Mostly.

Big Al laughed.

That was significantly less reassuring.

He considered asking more questions.

Then decided not to.

He trusted Elias.

If something was wrong, he'd find out soon enough.

At precisely six o'clock there was a knock at the apartment door.

Big Al immediately knew something was wrong.

Elias was never on time.

Ever.

The man had spent most of his adult life arriving somewhere between ten minutes late and next Tuesday.

Sandy opened the door first.

A moment later she called out.

"Your cousin's here."

Big Al maneuvered his wheelchair into the living room.

Elias stood in the doorway.

Jeans.

Boots.

Light jacket.

Normal.

Entirely normal.

Which somehow made everything stranger.

"Elias."

"Big Al."

"How'd you get here?"

"I had help."

"That is not an answer."

"It's the only answer you're getting."

Five minutes later they were outside.

The Southern California evening was warm.

A light breeze moved through the parking lot.

The cousins sat near the edge of the apartment complex where they could talk privately.

For a minute neither spoke.

Then Big Al pointed.

"Okay."

"Okay?"

"You needed privacy."

"Yes."

"You look like a guy trying very hard not to smile."

Elias failed immediately.

The grin appeared.

"Got me."

"I knew it."

"What?"

"You're up to something."

"I am."

Big Al sighed.

"Every time you say that, my life gets weirder."

"Today is going to set a new record."

A metallic sphere the size of a bowling ball appeared in midair.

Just appeared.

One moment nothing occupied the space beside them.

The next moment something else did.

Big Al stared.

Then blinked.

Then stared some more.

The sphere floated serenely three feet above the pavement.

Completely silent.

Completely impossible.

"Well ... damn."

The word emerged several seconds later.

"That's new."

The sphere rotated slightly.

"Hello, Alden."

Big Al looked at Elias.

Then at the orb.

Then back at Elias.

"Elias."

"Yeah?"

"What the hell is that?"

The grin became even wider.

"Big Al, I'd like you to meet my friend Opi."

The orb brightened slightly.

"Hello."

Big Al considered this.

For approximately three seconds.

Then nodded.

"Alien?"

"Yes."

"Actual alien?"

"Yes."

"Space alien?"

"Yes."

Big Al looked at Opi.

Then at Elias.

Then back at Opi.

"Okay."

Elias blinked.

"Okay?"

"Sure."

"Sure?"

Big Al shrugged.

"I've known you for forty years."

"Your point?"

"I think that is self-explanatory."

The orb laughed.

Elias pointed.

"See? This is why nobody likes you."

"My wife likes me."

"Your wife has questionable judgment."

For the next five minutes they talked.

Or more accurately, Big Al asked questions.

Lots of questions.

Most of them sensible.

Some of them absolutely not.

Opi answered what she could.

Elias answered what he understood.

Which was admittedly less.

Eventually Big Al sat back in his chair.

"I have officially reached my daily limit for impossible things."

"I hope not."

"Why?"

"Because now we get to the important part."

Big Al immediately became suspicious.

"That sentence concerns me."

"It should."

The suspicion intensified.

Elias looked at his cousin.

Really looked at him.

The chair.

The missing limbs.

The years of pain.

The decades of adaptation.

Everything.

Then he smiled.

A different smile this time.

Gentler.

More serious.

"Al."

"Yeah?"

"I need you to do something for me."

"What?"

"Close your eyes."

Big Al stared.

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because every terrible idea in human history started with somebody saying that."

Opi brightened.

"He has a point."

"Thank you."

Elias rolled his eyes.

"Do you trust me?"

The question lingered.

For all the jokes.

For all the sarcasm.

For all the years.

The answer was easy.

"Yes."

"Then close your eyes."

Big Al sighed.

"Fine."

His eyes closed.

"Now what?"

"Now nothing."

"This feels suspicious."

"It is suspicious."

"I knew it."

A circular opening appeared in the air beside them.

Not light.

Not energy.

Not a screen.

A hole.

A literal hole.

Through the opening stretched a green meadow.

Tall grass.

Pine trees.

Mountains in the distance.

A completely different world.

Big Al's eyes remained closed.

Fortunately.

Otherwise the explanation would have become considerably more difficult.

Elias moved behind the wheelchair.

Then began pushing.

The chair rolled smoothly forward.

Across the parking lot.

Toward the impossible opening.

Into it.

The temperature changed immediately.

Cooler.

Cleaner.

The scent of pine drifted through the air.

Birds called somewhere nearby.

The grass moved gently in the wind.

Elias stopped the chair.

The portal remained open behind them.

Opi floated silently overhead.

Waiting.

Watching.

Smiling.

At least as much as a floating metallic orb could smile.

Elias walked around in front of his cousin.

Then folded his arms.

"Okay."

Big Al opened one eye.

Then the other.

And for the first time in his life, Alden "Big Al" Mercer was completely speechless.

Big Al stared at the meadow.

Then at the pine trees.

Then at the mountains.

Then very slowly back at Elias.

"No."

Elias nodded.

"No."

"No no no."

"You may need a larger vocabulary."

Al looked behind him.

The parking lot in Southern California was still visible through the circular opening.

Beyond it, the apartment building.

The stucco wall.

The palm tree.

Sandy's window.

Then he looked forward again.

Grass.

Pine.

Mountains.

Cold clean air.

His mouth opened.

Closed.

Opened again.

Nothing came out.

Opi floated beside Elias, serene and annoyingly calm.

"Your physiological response is within normal parameters."

Al pointed at her.

"Do not medicalize my freak-out."

Elias laughed.

Opi brightened.

"I was attempting reassurance."

"You failed."

"Noted."

Al stared at the portal again.

"That's California."

"Yes."

"And this is Washington."

"Yes."

"And that is a hole in the air."

"Technically—"

Elias held up a hand.

"Opi."

"What?"

"Do not technically him right now."

The orb paused.

Al kept staring.

After nearly a minute he shook his head.

"Nope."

Then again.

"Nope."

Then finally he exhaled.

"Okay."

Elias blinked.

"Okay?"

"No."

Al pointed at the portal.

"Not okay. Nothing about that is okay. But I am choosing not to have a stroke."

"Good choice."

"I thought so."

Opi's light warmed.

"Teleportation is completely safe."

Al rubbed his face with his one damaged hand.

"I need the idiot version."

Elias crouched slightly so they were eye level.

"Opi can open doors between places."

"Doors."

"Basically."

"Through space."

"Basically."

He shook his head again.

The portal vanished.

No sound.

No flash.

One moment Southern California existed behind them.

The next there was only meadow.

Big Al stared at the empty air.

"That does not help."

"No," Elias said. "It probably doesn't."

It took Al several minutes to calm down.

He sat in his chair on the edge of a meadow in Stevens County, Washington, with his cousin standing beside him and a floating alien sphere overhead, trying to reorganize his understanding of reality.

It was not going well.

Finally he looked at Elias.

"Tell me this isn't the only trick."

Elias's expression changed.

Not much.

Just enough.

"No."

Al's humor faded.

"No?"

"No."

Elias looked at Opi.

Then back at Al.

"Not even close."

The meadow became quiet.

Wind moved through the grass.

Al suddenly felt the conversation shift beneath him.

This was no longer about teleportation.

This was something else.

Elias stepped closer.

"I want to show you something."

"Of course you do."

"Hold out your hand."

Al looked down at it.

His one hand.

The damaged one.

The fingers were short.

Crooked.

Useful enough for some things.

Useless for many others.

The hand had been part of him so long he rarely thought of it as damaged.

It was just his hand.

The only one he'd ever had.

He looked back up.

"Elias."

"I understand."

"Do you?"

"Yeah."

Al studied his cousin's face.

There was no grin now.

No joke.

No sarcasm.

Just Elias.

Steady.

Nervous.

Hopeful.

Terrified.

That frightened Al more than the portal.

"Ready?" Elias asked.

Al laughed once.

A short sound.

"Of course not."

Elias nodded.

Then looked at Opi.

"Do it."

Opi's light shifted.

Soft gold.

Then white.

Then something Al couldn't name.

There was no pain.

That was the first surprise.

No pain.

Only warmth.

A deep warmth that moved through his wrist, palm, fingers, bones, nerves, skin.

The second surprise was pressure.

Not unpleasant.

Just full.

Like something long-collapsed inside him was unfolding.

Al stared.

His fingers lengthened.

Straightened.

His palm reshaped.

Bones moved beneath skin without tearing it.

Nails formed.

Knuckles appeared.

Skin smoothed.

Tendons flexed.

A lifetime of wrongness corrected itself in less than 5 seconds.

Then it was done.

His hand was whole.

Not artificial.

Not prosthetic.

Not repaired badly.

Whole.

Al did not speak.

He lifted the hand.

Turned it.

Opened the fingers.

Closed them.

Opened them again.

The motion was so simple that the universe should have been embarrassed for denying it to him for forty-seven years.

His breath caught.

Elias said nothing.

Opi said nothing.

For once, both of them were wise enough to shut up.

Al touched his thumb to each finger.

One by one.

Then again.

Then again.

A sound escaped him.

Not quite laughter.

Not quite a sob.

Something in between.

Finally he whispered, "Holy shit."

Elias nodded.

"Yeah."

Al looked at him.

Then at Opi.

Then back at the hand.

"Can she..."

The question died.

He couldn't say it.

Couldn't force the words out.

Elias answered anyway.

"Yes."

Al's eyes lifted.

"All of me?"

"Yes."

"Arms?"

"Yes."

"Legs?"

"Yes."

"My back?"

"Yes."

"The rods?"

"She can remove them."

"Pain?"

"Yes."

"Everything?"

Elias looked to Opi.

Opi answered.

"Most of the structural, neurological, musculoskeletal, and systemic consequences of your congenital formation defects and subsequent compensatory damage can be repaired."

Al stared at her.

Elias sighed.

"She means yes."

"I did mean yes," Opi said.

"You used thirty-six words."

"I was being accurate."

"Try mercy next time."

Al was still staring at his hand.

Then suddenly he laughed.

Then stopped.

Then laughed again.

Then looked terrified.

The reactions chased one another across his face too quickly to name.

Hope.

Fear.

Disbelief.

Grief.

Anger.

Joy.

All of it.

All at once.

Elias waited.

Eventually Al looked up.

"Why haven't you done it already?"

"Because it is your body."

That answer came from Opi.

Immediate.

Certain.

Velhari.

Al looked at her.

She continued.

"No one may make this decision for you."

Elias nodded.

"And because there's a but."

Al swallowed.

"There's a but ?"

"Yeah."

Elias crouched in front of him again.

"Al, if you agree to this, it will smash your old life to smithereens."

Al looked at his new hand.

Then slowly back to him.

"What old life?"

"The one you understand."

That landed.

Elias continued.

"You can't just go back to work Monday and pretend nothing happened."

"No."

"You can't roll into your office with arms and legs and say you had a good weekend."

Despite everything, Al laughed.

"That would be a conversation."

"It would be ten thousand conversations."

"You'd need to move. Far away from everyone who knows the old you."

Al's smile faded.

"Why?"

"Because if people put two and two together, they won't call this medical treatment."

Elias pointed at the hand.

"They'll call it a miracle."

Al said nothing.

"And you know exactly how much trouble that will cause."

Al looked down.

He did know.

Everyone would want answers.

Doctors.

News stations.

Churches.

Governments.

Desperate people.

Crazy people.

Greedy people.

Dangerous people.

The thought spread outward, ugly and fast.

Elias let him see it.

Then added quietly:

"And that's not the worst part."

Al looked up.

"What is?"

"Sandy."

The name froze the air between them.

Elias did not soften it.

"What do you think she will think?"

Al's hand slowly closed.

"What do you think she will feel?"

"Eli—"

"What do you think she will do when the man she married becomes someone else?"

Al flinched.

Not physically.

Deeper than that.

Elias saw it and hated himself a little.

But he kept going.

Because someone had to.

"You and Sandy have a life. A real one. She loves you. But she loves the you she knows. The you she built that life with."

Al's jaw tightened.

"Opi can fix my body."

"Yes."

"But she cannot make Sandy okay with it."

"No."

Opi's light dimmed slightly.

"I cannot repair every consequence of transformation."

Elias glanced at her.

"That's a good sentence."

"I have been practicing."

Al didn't laugh.

Not this time.

He stared across the meadow.

At the mountains.

At the grass.

At a world impossibly far from the apartment he had left minutes ago.

Finally he said, "You think she'll leave me."

"I have no idea."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only honest one."

Al looked at him.

Elias held his gaze.

"I think it will be chaos."

"For both of you."

"For all of you."

"For everyone close to you."

Al looked down at the hand again.

Perfect.

Impossible.

His.

Elias's voice softened.

"So you need to think."

Al let out a breath.

"A lot."

"Yeah."

"A lot a lot."

"Probably more than that."

Opi floated closer.

"We can perform a demonstration for Sandy when you are ready."

Al looked at her.

"A demonstration?"

"Something limited. Clear. Reversible in its social implications."

Elias translated.

"Something that proves you're not on crack."

Al laughed despite himself.

"Thank you. I understood that version."

"Most humans do," Opi said.

Elias smiled.

"Then after that, you and Sandy talk. Really talk. Then you decide what you want your new life to look like."

Al's expression changed at the phrase.

New life.

Not improved life.

Not fixed life.

New life.

"That's a hell of a thing to say to somebody."

"I know."

"You bring me through a hole in the universe, introduce me to a floating alien, fix my hand, and then tell me to be careful."

"Pretty much."

Al shook his head.

"Your sales technique needs work."

Opi brightened.

"I told him that."

"Thank you, alien bowling ball."

"You are welcome."

For a long moment the three of them stood in the meadow.

The impossible hand.

The impossible offer.

The impossible cost.

Then Big Al looked at Elias.

Really looked at him.

And for the first time that day, his voice shook.

"If I say yes?"

Elias took a breath.

"Then we start figuring out how to give you a life big enough for the new you."

Al looked toward the mountains.

Then down at his hand.

Then at the place where the portal had been.

He had woken that morning expecting a normal day.

Now his entire life had split into before and after.

And the strangest part was not the alien.

Or the teleportation.

Or even the hand.

The strangest part was that for the first time in forty-seven years, Alden Mercer had to imagine a future without limits.

He was not sure he knew how.

Before they took Big Al back to his wife, Elias explained reluctantly that it probably would be a good idea to put his hand back in the orginal condition. Otherwise he wasn't going to be sleeping tonight when his wife saw the alteration.

After they bundled Big Al back to southern California with his original hand, before they left Elias simply said Good Luck Cuz. Call me if you need any advice or help. I'll wait to hear from you about what the next step should be.

The text arrived at 7:14 the following morning.

Elias was halfway through his first coffee.

Big Al:

Okay. Let's do this.

Elias stared at the message.

Then laughed.

Opi looked up from whatever impossible calculation occupied her attention.

"What?"

"He lasted less than twenty-four hours."

"That is longer than I expected."

A moment later another message arrived.

Big Al:

Sandy says if I don't do it she's going to kill me herself.

But she needs to see real proof that she isnt married to a lunatic first.

Opi brightened.

"A supportive spouse."

By late afternoon they were back in Southern California.

This time nobody bothered with surprises.

The portal opened in Big Al's living room.

Sandy stared at it.

Then at Opi.

Then at Elias.

Then back at the portal.

Unlike her husband, she immediately skipped several stages of disbelief.

"Alien?"

"Yes," Elias said.

"Actual alien?"

"Yes."

"Good."

Elias blinked.

"Good?"

"That saves time."

Opi brightened.

"I like her."

"So does everybody," Big Al said.

The demonstration proceeded almost exactly as it had in Stevens County.

Sandy watched.

Questioned.

Doubted.

Demanded evidence.

Then demanded more evidence.

Then attempted to explain what she was seeing.

Then abandoned the effort.

Eventually Opi repaired an old shoulder injury Sandy had lived with for nearly fifteen years.

The pain vanished instantly.

Sandy rotated her arm.

Then rotated it again.

Then again.

For nearly a minute she said nothing.

Finally:

"Oh."

Elias smiled.

"Yeah."

"Oh."

"That was my reaction too."

"No."

She continued moving the shoulder.

"You don't understand."

"Probably not."

"The pain is gone."

The room became quiet.

Because everybody understood exactly what she meant.

An hour later the excitement finally subsided.

Big Al sat silently in his wheelchair.

Sandy sat beside him.

Their hands intertwined.

Neither spoke.

Neither quite knew what to say.

The future had arrived.

It was sitting in their living room.

Floating, technically.

But still.

Elias eventually stood.

"We should go."

Neither Al nor Sandy reacted.

Both remained deep in thought.

Opi floated slightly forward.

"There are practical matters."

That got their attention.

"Such as?" Sandy asked.

Opi's light shifted.

"Documentation."

"Identity."

"Financial assets."

"Employment."

"Housing."

Big Al frowned.

"You can fix those?"

"Yes."

The answer came immediately.

Sandy looked skeptical.

"All of them?"

"Yes."

"How?"

Opi paused.

"The detailed explanation would require several days."

"Pass."

"A wise decision."

Big Al looked down at his chair.

Then back at Opi.

"What about records?"

"Medical records can be modified."

"Government records can be modified."

"Employment records can be modified."

The orb tilted slightly.

"History is surprisingly editable."

Elias snorted.

"That sentence should concern everyone."

For a few moments nobody spoke.

Then Opi continued.

"There is one complication."

The room became still.

Sandy immediately noticed the wording.

"One?"

"One major complication."

"Okay."

Opi's light softened.

"Your family."

The silence was immediate.

Big Al looked at Sandy.

Sandy looked at Big Al.

Nobody liked where this was going.

Opi continued gently.

"People who know Alden Mercer will immediately recognize that something impossible has occurred."

"No kidding," Big Al said.

The orb ignored him.

"Sandy's family cannot see the restored version of Alden."

The words landed heavily.

Sandy looked away first.

Because she knew Opi was right.

"They'd figure it out."

"Yes."

"They'd tell people."

"Yes."

"They wouldn't mean any harm."

"I know."

The room became quiet again.

Sometimes the truth was unpleasant.

Even when spoken kindly.

Elias finally stepped in.

"Look."

Everyone turned toward him.

"I've been thinking about this."

Opi brightened.

"Come to Stevens County."

Big Al blinked.

"What?"

"Come stay with me."

Sandy looked surprised.

"For how long?"

"A while."

The answer was honest.

Not precise.

Honest.

"Long enough to figure things out."

Elias turned to Opi and said

"I am guessing even if your create a new version there is going to be some learning and exercsise involved ?

I cant imagine a guy who has never walked before is going to be able to come running with me next week.

Opi : YOU are correct.

Sandy : So that means I have to teach another baby how to walk ?

Big Al snorted at that.

Elias pointed at the couple.

"You don't have to decide everything today."

"Or tomorrow."

"Or next week."

The humor disappeared.

"What you are being offered is bigger than a medical procedure."

Nobody argued.

Because nobody could.

"It changes everything."

He pointed at Al's wheelchair.

"The chair."

"The pain."

"The job."

"The future."

Then toward Sandy.

"The marriage."

"The family."

"The plans."

"Everything."

For a long moment nobody spoke.

Finally Sandy squeezed her husband's hand.

"What do you want?"

The question wasn't directed at Elias.

Or Opi.

Only Big Al.

His answer came surprisingly quickly.

"I want a chance."

Sandy nodded slowly.

"So do I."

The room fell silent once more.

Not uncomfortable.

Not fearful.

Just thoughtful.

The kind of silence that appeared when an old life was ending and a new one had not quite begun.

Elias glanced at Opi.

The orb's light glowed softly.

Satisfied.

Hopeful.

Very Velhari.

For once neither of them spoke.

There was nothing left to say.

The decision belonged to Al and Sandy now.

And that was exactly how it should be.
