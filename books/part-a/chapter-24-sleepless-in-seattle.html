Chapter 24: Sleepless in Seattle

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

The barn was cold.

Elias had stopped noticing.

Three empty coffee mugs sat on the workbench.

A legal pad covered in notes occupied the remaining space.

Opi floated nearby.

Her light pulsed slowly.

Amber.

Thinking.

"Seven hundred and eighty thousand."

Elias said it for perhaps the tenth time.

"Homeless individuals in the United States," Opi replied.

"Nineteen thousand in Seattle."

"Approximately."

Elias stared at the map.

Then at the notes.

Then back at the map.

Finally he sighed.

"I hate this."

"That is not a useful observation."

"It is if you're me."

The orb brightened slightly.

"Explain."

Elias stood and began pacing.

He did his best thinking while moving.

"Every instinct I have says go faster."

"Mine as well."

"Which worries me."

"Why?"

"Because when everyone agrees with me, I start looking for the mistake."

Opi considered that.

"A reasonable survival strategy."

"Thanks."

He pointed at Seattle.

"Nineteen thousand people."

"Yes."

"We could start tonight."

"Yes."

"We could probably move faster than any humanitarian operation in human history."

"Also yes."

Elias stopped pacing.

Then jabbed a finger at the map.

"And that's exactly how we screw this up."

The orb became still.

"Explain."

"I spent thirty years building software."

Opi made a noise.

"I am aware."

"No you're not."

That got her attention.

"I reviewed your entire professional history."

"You reviewed the data."

Elias pointed at her.

"Different thing."

The orb brightened.

Skeptical.

"Continue."

"We'd build something."

He began pacing again.

"Testing looked great."

"Beta looked great."

"Early users loved it."

"So we'd roll it out to everybody."

Opi already knew where this was going.

"Elias—"

"And then it exploded."

The orb sighed.

"I dislike where this analogy is heading."

"It is heading somewhere useful."

He stopped.

Turned.

Pointed at Seattle again.

"Nineteen thousand users."

"They are not users."

"You know what I mean."

"I do."

"Have you ever actually done this before?"

The orb hesitated.

That hesitation told him everything.

"No."

"No."

"You've observed humanity for nine thousand years."

"Correct."

"You've modeled humanity."

"Correct."

"You've analyzed humanity."

"Correct."

Elias folded his arms.

"But you've never actually changed a living human being."

The silence lasted several seconds.

Finally:

"No."

"Neither have I."

The orb brightened.

"You have influenced many humans."

"Not like this."

Elias shook his head.

"We are talking about rebuilding lives."

"Health."

"Housing."

"Purpose."

"Community."

"Meaning."

"We don't know what we don't know."

The orb remained silent.

Which usually meant she was listening.

"If we screw up one person?"

Elias shrugged.

"That's bad."

"If we screw up nineteen thousand people?"

The silence returned.

Longer this time.

Finally Opi spoke.

"The suffering occurring tonight while we discuss methodology is considerable."

Elias stopped pacing.

The tone made him look up immediately.

"Opi."

The orb brightened.

"What?"

"Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Use suffering as a rhetorical weapon."

The barn became very quiet.

Opi's light dimmed slightly.

"It was effective."

"It was manipulation."

Another pause.

Longer this time.

Then:

"Yes."

The answer was quiet.

"Yes, it was."

Elias nodded.

"You're smarter than that."

"I know."

"I know."

For several moments neither spoke.

Then Opi said:

"I dislike being wrong."

"Welcome to humanity."

"I am not human."

"Then you're getting the premium experience."

To his surprise the orb laughed.

A warm Velhari laugh.

"I dislike you occasionally."

"Good."

"Why?"

"Because it means you're learning."

The orb considered that.

Annoyingly, she suspected he was correct.

Again.

"I assume you have an alternative?"

"Of course."

Elias sat back down.

Picked up his pen.

And smiled.

The smile Opi was beginning to associate with trouble.

"We start with one."

The orb immediately brightened.

"No."

"See?"

"You have not even explained the idea."

"Don't need to."

"No."

"You hate it already."

"I do."

"Excellent."

"Elias—"

"One person."

The orb floated closer.

"Nineteen thousand people require help."

"I know."

"One person helps one person."

"Correct."

"This appears mathematically defective."

Elias grinned.

"There it is."

"There what is?"

"The alien."

The orb brightened.

"I am literally an alien."

"The part that thinks humans are spreadsheets."

"We are not spreadsheets."

"Exactly."

Elias tapped the map.

"Before we save civilization."

Tap.

"We need proof."

Tap.

"One success."

The orb became quiet.

Thinking.

Calculating.

Modeling.

Eventually she asked:

"Not random."

"No."

"Highly visible."

"Yes."

"Someone influential."

"Yes."

"Someone whose success changes the behavior of others."

Elias smiled.

Now they were thinking together.

"Exactly."

The orb brightened.

Gold now.

Interested.

"I have twenty-three candidates."

"Good."

"One is particularly interesting."

"Tell me."

The image of tall black man appeared above the workbench.

Elias studied the face.

Then smiled.

"There you are."

Opi tilted slightly.

"You know him already?"

"No."

Elias leaned forward.

"But I know that look."

"What look?"

"The guy everybody follows."

The orb brightened.

Marcus Webb.

Former union organizer.

Natural leader.

Four years homeless.

And quite possibly the first domino.

The image of Marcus Webb hovered above the workbench.

Beside it appeared dozens of smaller faces.

Relationships.

Connections.

Friendships.

Arguments.

Dependencies.

The social structure of the encampment.

Elias studied it.

For a while neither spoke.

That wasn't unusual.

Opi had discovered that when Elias became quiet, interesting things frequently happened.

Eventually he pointed.

"Who's that?"

A woman's image expanded.

Hispanic.

Early sixties.

Tired eyes.

The kind of face that had spent years carrying more than its share of problems.

"Gloria Martinez."

"What about her?"

"Type Two diabetes."

"Progressive kidney damage."

"Neuropathy."

"Hypertension."

"Malnutrition."

The list continued.

Elias held up a hand.

"No."

The orb paused.

"No?"

"That's not what I asked."

Opi brightened slightly.

Then recalibrated.

"What are you asking?"

Elias pointed at the connection between Gloria and Marcus.

"Why is that line thicker than the others?"

The map shifted.

Several statistics appeared.

Interaction frequency.

Conversation duration.

Physical proximity.

Observed behavior.

"Marcus checks on her daily."

"How daily?"

"Every day."

Elias smiled.

"There it is."

"There what is?"

"Our opening."

The orb became very still.

For several seconds she said nothing.

Then:

"Interesting."

Elias laughed.

"You hate that."

"I do not."

"You absolutely do."

The orb brightened.

"I dislike when humans reach correct conclusions using incomplete information."

"That's called intuition."

"It is an inefficient process."

"It has worked out pretty well so far."

Opi reluctantly admitted this was true.

Elias stood and resumed pacing.

"Let's think this through."

The orb projected additional information.

Gloria's medical history appeared.

Not pleasant reading.

"She's sick."

"Very."

"How long before she loses the foot?"

Opi processed several models.

"Current probability suggests between two and four months."

Elias winced.

"And Marcus knows it."

"Yes."

"He worries about her."

"Yes."

"He can't help her."

"No."

Elias stopped pacing.

The smile appeared.

The one Opi was learning to recognize.

The smile of a software consultant who had found the bug.

"Oh, that's nasty."

The orb brightened.

"What is?"

"We don't approach Marcus."

Silence.

Then:

"Yeah ?"

"Yeah."

For a few moments neither spoke.

Then Opi began running simulations.

Thousands.

Tens of thousands.

The probabilities shifted rapidly.

Curiosity.

Trust.

Engagement.

Influence.

Network effects.

The numbers improved dramatically.

Very dramatically.

"Elias."

"Hmm?"

"This increases the probability of successful engagement by sixty-seven percent."

"Only sixty-seven?"

The orb brightened.

"That is an enormous improvement."

"Good."

"It is also somewhat manipulative."

Elias sat back down.

"No."

The orb tilted slightly.

"No?"

"We aren't tricking Marcus."

"We are influencing him."

"We aren't helping Gloria just because Marcus will notice."

"We're helping Gloria because Gloria needs help. Marcus noticing is a hopeful side effect"

And if it fails, Gloria still has a better life and we can try something else with Marcus.

Not a lot of downside in my opinion.

Eventually:

"You would help her even if Marcus did not exist."

"Of course."

The answer came instantly.

The orb's light shifted.

Softer now.

Thoughtful.

"Then the action is ethical."

"Welcome to humanity."

"I continue to dislike how your species arrives at conclusions."

"That's because we're not spreadsheets."

The image of Gloria rotated slowly above the table.

Elias studied her face.

"Tell me about her."

Opi complied.

Age.

Health.

History.

Employment.

Family.

A life reduced to data.

By the end of it Elias was frowning.

"What?"

"She got screwed."

"That is not a clinical assessment."

"It is an accurate assessment."

The orb considered this.

"Yes."

"It is."

Several minutes passed.

Finally Elias pointed at Gloria's image.

"We start with her."

The orb brightened.

"Agreed."

"No speeches."

"Agreed."

"No recruiting."

"Agreed."

"No civilization-saving discussions."

"Agreed."

"We fix her."

The orb floated slightly higher.

"And then?"

Elias grinned.

The grin looked remarkably similar to the one he'd worn before launching software products that disrupted entire industries.

"Then we wait."

"For what?"

He pointed at Marcus Webb.

"We wait for him."

The orb became very still.

Then a warm gold.

Amusement.

Recognition.

Understanding.

Because Marcus Webb was exactly the sort of man who would ignore promises.

Ignore theories.

Ignore strangers.

But he would absolutely investigate a miracle.

And unlike most miracles...

This one was real.

The person Marcus cared about most in the camp wasn't his closest friend.

It was an older woman named Gloria.

Sixty-two.

Diabetic.

Living in a tent that leaks whenever it rains.

One of the few people in the encampment who still tries to keep herself neat.

Marcus checks on her every day.

Not because anyone asked him to.

Because somebody has to.

Three days earlier Gloria had developed a serious infection in her left foot.

Marcus knew it.

Gloria knew it.

Everyone knew it.

Nobody could do much about it.

The emergency room would patch her up and send her back.

Assuming she could even get there.

Marcus had been quietly preparing himself for the possibility that she might lose the foot.

Or worse.

Then something strange happened.

She disappeared.

Not permanently.

Just for one afternoon.

When she came back she was smiling.

Actually smiling.

Marcus couldn't remember the last time he'd seen that.

"Where were you?"

"Doctor."

"What doctor?"

She shrugged.

"Some guy."

"What guy?"

"The nice & smart one."

"That's helpful."

"The alien was there too."

Marcus stared at her.

"The what?"

"The alien."

"Gloria."

"I'm serious."

Marcus decided the infection had probably reached her brain.

The next morning Gloria walked across the camp carrying two grocery bags.

Walking normally.

No limp.

No pain.

No bandages.

Nothing.

Marcus watched her approach.

Something cold settled into his stomach.

Because he knew what he had seen two days earlier.

And this wasn't possible.

"Show me your foot."

Gloria laughed.

"Buy me dinner first."

"Gloria."

She sat down and removed her shoe.

Marcus stared.

The infection was gone.

Not improved.

Gone.

The skin looked healthier than it had in years.

He slowly looked up.

"Who did this?"

She pointed.

Across the street.

Near a small coffee stand.

A man sat at a table drinking coffee.

Medium height, about 50 years old, thin, fit, not rich looking - looking like a slightly younger version of that actor who played that astronaut guy in that old movie - what was his name ? ah .... Ed Harris. Bald on top, thin face, but not a guy to mess with Marcus thought.

Marcus crossed the street.

The man looked up.

"Marcus Webb."

Marcus didn't sit.

"Who are you?"

"Elias Mercer."

"How'd you fix Gloria's foot?"

Elias pointed at the empty chair.

Marcus remained standing.

Elias nodded.

"Fair enough."

"Answer the question."

"I helped."

"Nobody helps like that."

"You're right."

Marcus studied him.

Everything about the man felt wrong.

Not dangerous.

Not threatening.

Just...

Certain.

Like somebody who already knew how the conversation would end.

"Why Gloria?"

Marcus asked.

"Because she needed help."

Marcus shook his head.

"No."

His voice hardened.

"Why Gloria?"

Elias smiled slightly.

Now they were getting somewhere.

"Because," he said quietly, "I knew if I helped you, you'd wonder what I wanted."

Marcus said nothing.

"Helping Gloria was more efficient."

For the first time Marcus almost laughed.

Against his will.

A little bark of amusement escaped.

"That's your plan?"

"It appears to be working."

Marcus stared at him.

Then finally sat down.

Not because he trusted him.

Not even close.

But because for the first time in years something genuinely impossible had happened.

And because anybody who could give Gloria her foot back deserved at least one conversation.

As Marcus lowered himself into the chair, a small sphere about the size of a bowling ball drifted silently around the corner of the building and stopped right beside Elias where no passerbys could see it.

Marcus blinked once.

Twice.

Then looked at Elias.

Then looked at the sphere.

Then back at Elias.

Elias took a sip of coffee.

"That's Opi."

Marcus rubbed his face.

"Of course it is."

The sphere spoke.

"Good morning, Marcus."

Marcus stared at it for several seconds.

Then sighed.

"You know what?"

"What?"

"If Gloria starts levitating too, I'm leaving."

For the first time, Opi laughed.

And despite himself, Marcus smiled.

-- MARCUS

Sodo Encampment, Seattle — 3:14 AM

Marcus Webb woke up the way he always did — fast and afraid, heart hammering, reaching for Carol before remembering.

Always remembering.

He lay in the dark of his tent listening to the rain on the nylon and the distant sound of the interstate and did the thing he did every night where he tried to find one reason to get up in the morning.

He was still looking when he felt it.

Not pain. The opposite of pain. A warmth that started in the center of his chest and moved outward slowly like the first swallow of something good. He sat up. Put his hand on his sternum. His heart was beating differently — not faster, not slower, just cleaner somehow. Like something that had been slightly wrong for a very long time had been quietly corrected.

In a small building in the warehouse district, Opi & Elias sat in their newly purchased building and watched Marcus on a series of projections opi had created which showed the entire scene from different angles and perspectives. in other views there were real time feeds of all of Marcus's vitals. It was like some kind of futuristic high tech hospital control room.

"What are you doing?" he whispered.

"His cortisol levels were at chronic crisis elevation," Opi said quietly. "Four years of sustained grief and trauma had dysregulated his neurological baseline so severely that he was physiologically incapable of feeling safe regardless of external circumstances. I have recalibrated his autonomic nervous system to its pre-loss baseline. He will not know what happened. He will only know that something has shifted."

"Is that —" Elias stopped. "Did he consent to that?"

"He has wished to feel better every day for four years," Opi said. "I am choosing to consider that consent. You may challenge me on this later. Right now watch."

In the tent Marcus sat very still for a long time.

Then he picked up his phone — cracked screen, 4% battery — and opened it for the first time to the voicemail from his sister Linda that he'd never listened to.

He didn't know why. He just did it.

Her voice came out of the speaker and she was crying a little and she said Marcus I'm not calling to fix anything I'm just calling because I miss my brother and whenever you're ready I'm here and then it ended.

He sat with that for a while.

Then Opi moved.

Into his phone. Into every database simultaneously. DMV, Social Security, banks, King County housing registry, VA records, criminal background — she didn't erase his history, she clarified it, cleaned it, highlighting every strength, every qualification, every legitimate pathway that bureaucratic noise had buried. In less than a second she completed what would have taken a team of dedicated professionals four weeks.

His phone buzzed. A text from an unknown number:

"Studio apartment. Beacon Hill. Already approved and prepaid for the next 6 months. Your name on the lease. Landlord is Gerald Fitch. Call him at 8am. He's expecting you."

Marcus stared at it.

"Who is this?" he typed back.

The reply came immediately: Opi and Elias.

Marcus re-read the original message twice. Then he did something he hadn't done in four years.

He made a plan for the day to come.
