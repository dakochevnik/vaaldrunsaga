Chapter 16: Dana Mercer

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 16 of 32

← Chapter 15Part A IndexChapter 17 →

Dana Mercer stood alone in the kitchen.

The house was quiet.

That wasn't unusual.

Elias had always preferred quiet.

The man could sit on a porch for three hours staring at trees and somehow consider that a productive use of time.

Dana smiled despite herself.

That had been one of the things she most liked about him once. Definitely not a needy husband.

Maybe she still did.

The problem wasn't love.

The problem was everything else.

She looked around the room.

The kitchen table.

The worn cabinets.

The coffee mug Elias had forgotten on the counter.

Fifteen years.

Fifteen years was a long time to build a life.

Long enough to forget what your original plans had been.

Long enough to become someone else.

When she met Elias outside Phoenix she had been twenty-four years old.

He had been older.

Smarter.

Confident in a way most men weren't.

Not loud.

Not flashy.

The exact opposite.

The first thing she noticed was that he didn't seem impressed by anybody.

Managers.

Executives.

Government officials.

Millionaires.

They all got treated exactly the same.

At the time she found that fascinating.

Most people spent their lives chasing status.

Elias seemed almost immune to it.

For years she thought that was wisdom.

Maybe it was.

But eventually she started wondering if it was also a limitation.

The years passed.

Cities changed.

Projects changed.

The children arrived.

Noah.

Then Claire.

The four of them built a good life together.

A stable life.

A safe life.

A respectable life.

And somehow that became the problem.

Dana sat down at the table.

The realization had taken years to fully form.

She wanted more.

More people.

More activity.

More experiences.

More movement.

More life.

Elias wanted less.

Less noise.

Less bureaucracy.

Less interference.

Less everything.

At first the difference seemed small.

Then it became impossible to ignore.

The final years felt like watching two roads slowly diverge.

Nobody had done anything wrong.

Nobody had betrayed anybody.

Nobody had stopped caring.

They were simply walking in different directions.

The sound of footsteps interrupted her thoughts.

Elias entered the kitchen.

Running clothes.

Coffee mug.

Barefoot.

The usual.

He looked at her.

She looked at him.

Neither spoke for several seconds.

Finally Elias sighed.

"Still leaving?"

Dana laughed softly.

"You already know the answer."

"I do."

He nodded.

Then sat across from her.

No anger.

No accusations.

No bargaining.

That somehow made it worse.

Most people fought for things they wanted.

Elias never had.

Not jobs.

Not money.

Not status.

Not people.

Especially not people.

"You could stop me."

The words escaped before she could stop them.

Elias looked genuinely confused.

"No I couldn't."

"Sure you could."

"No."

He shook his head.

"That's not how any of this works."

Dana stared at him.

There it was.

The thing that made him who he was.

The thing she admired.

The thing she hated.

The thing she would probably miss.

Agency.

Elias believed in it with almost religious certainty.

People made choices.

People lived with the consequences.

That was the deal.

Always.

"You know," she said quietly, "most husbands would at least try."

"I love you."

The response came instantly.

No hesitation.

No drama.

Just a statement of fact.

"I know."

"I don't want you to go."

She swallowed.

"I know."

"But I won't stop you."

Dana looked away.

Because of course he wouldn't.

That wasn't who he was.

The silence stretched between them.

Finally Elias reached into a drawer and placed an envelope on the table.

Dana frowned.

"What's this?"

"Your new life."

She opened it.

The check inside read fifty thousand dollars.

For a moment she couldn't speak.

"Elias..."

"You'll need a head start."

Dana stared at the number.

Then at the man sitting across from her.

The strange thing was that this was exactly the sort of thing he would do.

No anger.

No revenge.

No scorekeeping.

Just help.

Even now.

Especially now.

A lump formed in her throat.

"You make this very difficult."

"I know."

For the first time that evening Elias smiled.

A small smile.

The one she had always liked best.

"Fortunately," he said, "you've always been stubborn."

Dana laughed.

Then unexpectedly started crying.

Fifteen years.

Fifteen years reduced to a kitchen conversation.

Tomorrow she would leave for Las Vegas.

Tomorrow Noah and Claire would begin adjusting to a new reality.

Tomorrow her life would change.

Tonight she simply sat across from the man she had spent almost two decades loving.

And wondered how something could end without anybody being the villain.

That was the part nobody prepared you for.

Sometimes a marriage failed because somebody was cruel.

Sometimes because somebody was selfish.

Sometimes because somebody stopped caring.

And sometimes it failed because two decent people wanted different futures.

Those were the hardest ones.

Because there was nobody to blame.

Not even yourself.

Most of Elias's reaction was based on his almost religious belief in agency.

He had not fought to keep Dana because he respected her right to make her own choices.

If she wanted to create a new life, that was her decision to make.

Always.

Agency was sacred.

But agency was not merely about rights and freedoms.

It was balanced by an equal and opposite demand for responsibility.

Choices created consequences.

Good choices.

Bad choices.

Wise choices.

Stupid choices.

The universe did not particularly care which category they belonged to.

The bill always arrived eventually.

Even refusing to choose was still a choice.

If you don't believe that, try standing in front of a speeding bus and instead of running left or running right, justa make no choice at all.

Good luck with that.

Dana had made a choice more than a decade earlier.

She chose to become a mother.

Two children.

Noah and Claire.

The responsibility created by that decision did not magically disappear when she later chose a different future in Las Vegas.

Responsibilities were not coats you could hang in a closet when they became inconvenient.

For this, Elias could never forgive her.

He understood wanting a different life.

He understood regret.

He understood mistakes.

God knew he had made enough of them himself.

He was a grown man.

He had spent decades dealing with the consequences of his own decisions.

That was part of the deal.

Always.

But leaving two pre-teen children because your life felt too small, too predictable, too boring?

That was different.

The damage would not be measured in months.

Or years.

It would be measured across entire lives.

Noah would carry it.

Claire would carry it.

Long after everyone involved had convinced themselves they had moved on.

Elias never hated Dana.

In many ways he still loved her.

He hoped she found what she was looking for.

He hoped Las Vegas gave her the happiness she believed was waiting there.

He genuinely meant that.

But there were some debts in life that could not be transferred.

Some responsibilities that could not be abandoned.

And some choices that remained yours forever.

Whether you wanted them to or not.
