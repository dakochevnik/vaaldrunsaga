Chapter 25: Fernando Reyes

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 25 of 32

← Chapter 24Part A IndexChapter 26 →

Fernando Reyes sat in the driver's seat of his Honda Civic and listened to the engine make a noise it definitely wasn't supposed to make.

"Please don't."

The engine ignored him.

The rattling continued.

Fernando closed his eyes.

The car had two hundred and eighty thousand miles on it.

The check engine light had been on so long it felt like part of the dashboard.

The air conditioning didn't work.

One window wouldn't roll down.

The transmission occasionally made decisions without consulting him.

And now there was a new noise.

Wonderful.

He shut the engine off.

For a moment he simply sat there.

The DoorDash app showed another delivery.

Eight dollars and twenty-three cents.

Twenty-seven minutes.

Across town.

Fernando declined it.

He was tired.

Not physically.

Not completely.

Just tired.

Twenty-eight years old and everything felt harder than it was supposed to.

The jobs paid less.

The rent cost more.

The future looked smaller.

Nobody ever said it directly.

But everybody seemed to know.

The game wasn't working anymore.

His phone buzzed.

His mother.

Fernando answered.

"Hey Mom."

"Are you coming home for dinner?"

Home.

The word still bothered him.

Not because he disliked his mother.

He loved her.

Always would.

The problem was that a twenty-eight-year-old man wasn't supposed to live in his childhood bedroom.

At least that was what he had always believed.

Life had other plans.

"Yeah."

"Good."

A pause.

"You sound tired."

"I'm fine."

She didn't believe him.

Neither did he.

The house looked exactly the same as it had when he was twelve.

Same porch.

Same driveway.

Same maple tree.

Different future.

His mother hugged him when he came inside.

The smell of dinner filled the kitchen.

For a moment he was ten years old again.

Safe.

Protected.

Certain that adults understood what they were doing.

Then reality returned.

His mother asked about work.

Fernando lied.

"Busy."

She nodded.

The lie was familiar.

They both knew it.

Neither mentioned it.

Later that evening he found himself staring at an old family photograph.

His father stood beside his mother.

Both smiling.

Fernando remembered that day.

The camping trip.

The fishing.

The laughter.

The feeling that his family would last forever.

Instead the divorce came.

Then another relationship.

Then another.

Then another.

His father moved away.

His mother moved on.

Everyone kept searching for something better.

Fernando wasn't even sure what "better" meant anymore.

The photograph showed two imperfect people.

At least they had been together.

At least they had tried.

He sometimes wondered if modern life had become a machine that took ordinary families apart and then acted surprised when nobody could put them back together.

That night he sat on the back porch.

The neighborhood was quiet.

A few lights glowed in distant windows.

Families.

Couples.

Children.

Lives.

Fernando looked up at the stars.

When he was younger he had imagined a different future.

A wife.

Kids.

A house.

Nothing extravagant.

Just ordinary happiness.

The kind his grandparents seemed to have found without much effort.

Now even imagining it felt dangerous.

Hope was expensive.

Hope hurt when it failed.

And lately it always seemed to fail.

He rubbed his face.

Maybe things would improve.

Maybe next year.

Maybe after a better job.

Maybe after the car was fixed.

Maybe after something.

The problem was that every year seemed to begin with another maybe.

And end with another disappointment.

The sound of laughter drifted from a neighboring house.

Fernando listened quietly.

Then looked away.

For the first time in his life he found himself wondering whether some people simply weren't meant to build families of their own.

The thought frightened him.

Not because he believed it.

Because he was starting to.

And that was worse.

Much worse.

The porch grew darker as the evening wore on.

Eventually Fernando stood and headed inside.

Tomorrow would be another day.

Another delivery.

Another bill.

Another maybe.
