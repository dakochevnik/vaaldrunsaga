Chapter 13: Noah Mercer

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 13 of 32

← Chapter 12Part A IndexChapter 14 →

The alarm went off at 4:15 AM.

Noah Mercer was already awake.

Mostly because he hated alarms.

If something was going to drag him out of bed before sunrise, he'd rather beat it to the punch.

The apartment was dark.

Quiet.

Peaceful.

For another thirty seconds.

Then his girlfriend's phone started vibrating somewhere under a pile of blankets.

Noah grinned.

"Your army is calling."

A muffled voice emerged from beneath the blankets.

"No."

"I think it is."

"No."

"The United States government needs you."

"They can wait."

"They seem impatient."

A pillow emerged from the darkness and hit him.

Noah considered this a successful conversation.

He rolled out of bed and headed for the kitchen.

Coffee first.

Civilization second.

That had always seemed like the correct order.

The Tesla shuttle arrived at 4:54 AM.

Right on schedule.

Noah climbed aboard with several dozen other production associates.

Most looked half asleep.

A few were staring at phones.

One guy was already drinking an energy drink that appeared capable of removing paint.

Noah took a window seat.

The shuttle pulled away from the stop and joined the steady stream of traffic heading toward Gigafactory Texas.

The building appeared on the horizon forty-five minutes later.

Even after years of working there, the size still impressed him.

Factories weren't supposed to be that large.

The structure looked less like a building and more like somebody had accidentally constructed a small country.

People often talked about Tesla as if it were some abstract company.

For Noah it was simple.

It was where he went every day.

It was where thousands of people showed up before sunrise and spent twelve hours building something tangible.

Cars.

Battery packs.

The future.

Whatever label people wanted to use.

Noah mostly thought of it as work.

Good work.

The production floor was already alive when he arrived.

Noise.

Movement.

Machines.

Thousands of people all working toward the same goal.

Today started at one station.

Tomorrow would be another.

Tesla liked rotating associates through different jobs.

Some people hated it.

Noah didn't.

He enjoyed learning how things worked.

Every process.

Every machine.

Every problem.

Besides, standing in one place doing the exact same thing for twelve hours sounded like a special kind of torture.

A supervisor stopped beside him.

"Need another body on line three."

Noah nodded.

"No problem."

The supervisor smiled.

Noah got that response a lot.

Reliable people tended to.

Lunch found him sitting beside his sister.

Claire looked tired.

Noah immediately pointed it out.

"Rough morning?"

Claire pointed at him.

"You're annoying."

"Correct."

"You know exactly how annoying."

"Also correct."

Claire rolled her eyes.

The two siblings had spent their entire lives arguing like this.

Mostly because they enjoyed it.

Neither had inherited much patience.

Both had inherited far too much intelligence.

Their father called it a design flaw.

Claire called it genetics.

Noah called it entertainment.

Near the end of the shift his phone buzzed.

The monthly overtime schedule had opened.

Noah immediately pulled up the app.

Several extra shifts were available.

He selected two.

Tap.

Tap.

Done.

Twenty-four additional hours for the month.

His father had always been very direct about overtime.

If you're young, healthy, and somebody is willing to pay you extra money, you should probably take it.

Noah had listened.

The result was that at twenty-three years old he was earning nearly eighty thousand dollars a year despite never attending college.

Not bad.

Especially considering many people his age were carrying enough student debt to buy a decent pickup truck.

It got better everytime Noah opened his home finance app - besides doing OT, his father had convinced him of two other things, live frugally, cheaply as possible - no car, sharing an apartment with his sister and cooking his own food - then he stacked all the extra cash in treasury notes. The result of those decisions was that after 5 years at telsa he had a net worth of just under 350,000 dollars. He never talked about this to anyone other than his father. His big plan - when got closer to 500,000 was to quit tesla, move to china and learn mandarin. He could probably live fine for a long time just on the interest after that.

Claire was just one year behind him on the exact same financial trajectory.

The two siblings had inherited several things from their father.

An unfortunate tendency toward sarcasm.

And a powerful belief that if something needed doing, you should simply get on with it.

Noah locked his phone and smiled.

Two more shifts.

Good.

Future Noah would appreciate the decision.

Present Noah wasn't particularly worried about him.

The gym was crowded when he finally arrived.

Of course it was.

Every machine occupied.

Every bench taken.

Noah waited his turn.

Then got to work.

Running.

Weights.

More running.

The twelve-hour shift had already left him exhausted.

The workout finished the job.

Perfect.

His father had taught him something else growing up.

A tired body was usually a happy body.

Not always.

But usually.

That evening he called Elias.

His father answered immediately.

"What's broken?"

Noah laughed.

"Nothing."

"Then why are you calling?"

"Just saying hello."

A pause.

"Suspicious."

"No."

"Very suspicious."

Noah could practically hear the grin through the phone.

"How's Washington?"

"Still attached to Canada."

"How's Tesla?"

"Still making cars."

"Good."

Another pause.

Then Elias asked the real question.

"You taking the overtime?"

"Of course."

"Excellent."

"You're predictable."

"So are you."

The two men talked for another twenty minutes.

Work.

Family.

Random nonsense.

The usual.

When the call ended Noah felt better than before it started.

That was generally how conversations with his father worked.

The apartment was quiet when he got home.

His girlfriend had fallen asleep while studying.

One textbook rested on her chest.

Another was open beside her.

Noah carefully removed both.

The tiny reservist barely stirred.

At four foot nine she somehow occupied an amount of space that violated several laws of physics.

He covered her with a blanket.

Then sat quietly for a moment.

It had been a good day.

Not a great day.

Nothing extraordinary had happened.

Nobody had won a lottery.

Nobody had saved the world.

Just work.

Family.

Exercise.

A little progress.

A little purpose.

A little peace.

Noah smiled.

His father often said that success wasn't built from giant decisions.

It was built from thousands of ordinary days stacked carefully on top of one another.

Looking around the apartment, Noah suspected the old man might be right.

Noah turned off the light.

He had work in the morning.

Right on schedule.
