Chapter 17: Factory Brainstorming 3

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 17 of 32

← Chapter 16Part A IndexChapter 18 →

Elias stared at the whiteboard.

Then he slowly circled one phrase.

PAIR FORMATION FAILURE.

"There."

Opi floated closer.

"You believe that is the bottleneck?"

"I think it might be."

The orb remained silent.

A positive sign.

Silence usually meant she was thinking.

Elias continued.

"We've already established something important."

"What?"

"Men and women aren't matching."

"Agreed."

"Not at the rates necessary to maintain replacement fertility."

"Agreed."

He pointed at the board.

"So why?"

Opi projected another collection of demographic models.

"Many possible reasons."

"Give me the big ones."

The graphs shifted.

Attraction.

Income.

Education.

Status.

Geography.

Personality.

Shared values.

Timing.

Luck.

Everything interconnected.

Then Opi highlighted one category.

STATUS.

Elias nodded immediately.

"That's the one."

"Explain."

"Hypergamy."

The orb brightened.

"A commonly used term."

"An accurate one."

Opi considered that.

"Many studies do show that women often prefer partners who equal or exceed them in certain categories."

"Income."

"Frequently."

"Status."

"Frequently."

"Competence."

"Frequently."

"Ambition."

"Frequently."

"Social standing."

"Frequently."

Elias pointed triumphantly.

"Exactly."

"That observation alone does not create a problem."

"No."

"The problem emerges when distributions become misaligned."

Elias smiled.

Now they were getting somewhere.

"Say that again."

"The problem emerges when distributions become misaligned."

The orb projected two overlapping curves.

One represented men.

One represented women.

At first they aligned.

Then they slowly drifted apart.

The overlap shrank.

"So fewer matches."

"Correct."

"Fewer marriages."

"Correct."

"Fewer children."

"Correct."

Elias leaned back.

"That's it."

Opi tilted slightly.

"It?"

"The thing we've been looking for."

"What specifically?"

Elias stood and began pacing.

"The issue isn't attraction."

"No."

"The issue isn't sex."

"No."

"The issue isn't fertility."

"No."

"The issue is that the pool of people each group finds acceptable no longer overlaps enough."

The orb became still.

"Interesting."

Elias pointed toward the graph.

"If enough women seek men above themselves on several important dimensions..."

"Continue."

"And enough men fail to meet those thresholds..."

"Continue."

"Then pair formation declines."

The orb brightened.

"The mathematics support that possibility."

Elias laughed.

"Finally."

"Do not celebrate yet."

"Why not?"

"Because identifying a bottleneck is easier than solving one."

That was annoyingly true.

Elias sat back down.

"Okay."

"So let's assume we're right."

"Very well."

"We have a status mismatch."

"Potentially."

"We need more overlap."

"Potentially."

The orb projected another chart.

"What solutions exist?"

Elias immediately answered.

"Well."

He pointed at one side of the graph.

"You could lower women's status."

The orb became silent.

Then:

"Technically."

Elias burst out laughing.

"I was joking."

"I know."

"Please tell me you weren't seriously considering it."

"I calculated the scenario."

"Of course you did."

"It performs poorly."

"Good."

"Additionally, it would violate approximately four hundred and twelve principles we have already established."

The orb dimmed.

"Also it would be profoundly unfair."

"Exactly."

Elias shook his head.

"No."

"We're not pushing anybody down."

"Agreed."

The graph remained floating between them.

Which left only one option.

Opi reached the same conclusion at the same moment.

"Raise the status of men."

"Raise the status of men."

They both spoke simultaneously.

Then stopped.

The orb brightened.

"Interesting."

Elias stood again.

"No seriously."

He began pacing.

"What if that's the answer?"

"What if we've been looking at this backwards?"

"Explain."

"What if the objective isn't changing women at all?"

The orb remained silent.

"What if the objective is making more men attractive?"

Now Opi looked interested.

"Continue."

Elias pointed toward the valley below.

"The Parallel."

"What about it?"

"We're already doing it."

The orb froze.

A very unusual reaction.

"We're taking homeless men."

"Giving them housing."

"Giving them purpose."

"Giving them jobs."

"Giving them health."

"Giving them confidence."

"Giving them status."

One by one the words accumulated.

Opi slowly rotated.

The implication becoming obvious.

Elias felt a grin spreading across his face.

"Oh my God."

"What?"

"The villages."

The orb brightened.

"The villages."

"We thought we were solving homelessness."

"Yes."

"We're actually manufacturing husbands."

For almost three seconds Opi said absolutely nothing.

Then:

"That may be the most Elias Mercer sentence ever constructed."

"Thank you."

"It was not a compliment."

But neither of them cared.

Because for the first time all afternoon they had found something actionable.

Not a theory.

Not a complaint.

Not an ideology.

A lever.

A real lever.

If pair formation depended partly on competence, stability, confidence, purpose, health, and status—

then perhaps the solution was not forcing anyone downward.

Perhaps the solution was creating far more men who naturally moved upward.

And for the first time that day, the problem looked solvable.

Not easy.

Not quick.

But solvable.

Which, Elias reflected, was considerably better than most of the alternatives.
