Chapter 21: Ten Easy Steps (to save the World)

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 21 of 32

← Chapter 20Part A IndexChapter 22 →

The following morning Elias found himself staring at demographic charts again.

This annoyed him.

He had spent most of his life solving software problems, organizational problems, human problems, and occasionally problems caused by software organizations filled with humans. He had never once expected to spend his retirement studying birth rates.

Yet here he was.

The charts floated above the kitchen table while Opi hovered nearby.

"Help me understand something."

The orb brightened.

"That statement has historically led to interesting outcomes."

"I'm serious."

"So am I."

Elias pointed at the graphs.

"Let's assume you're right."

Opi remained silent.

"Let's assume your math is right."

Still silent.

"Let's assume the fertility collapse is real."

"Proceed."

"The mathematician in me wants to know whether the publicly available numbers are accurate."

The orb immediately answered.

"No."

That surprised him.

"No?"

"No."

"Too optimistic or too pessimistic?"

"Far too optimistic."

Elias leaned back.

"Why?"

Opi's lights shifted toward amber.

"Two reasons."

"First, politicians lie."

"That's hardly groundbreaking news."

"No."

"They lie because they believe panic is dangerous."

She paused.

"They also lie because admitting failure tends to shorten political careers."

"Fair."

"The second reason is more important."

"Which is?"

"The projections themselves are optimistic."

Elias frowned.

"Meaning?"

"The models assume stabilization."

"The models assume cultural adaptation."

"The models assume human behavior eventually changes the TFR in a positive manner."

The orb rotated slowly.

"My projections the opposite."

That landed heavily.

"Show me."

For several seconds Opi remained silent.

Then a map of South Korea appeared above the table.

Seoul glowed brightly.

"Let us discuss Seoul."

"Oh good."

"The total fertility rate in Seoul has approached approximately six-tenths of a child per woman."

Elias nodded.

He had seen the numbers.

They were horrifying.

What bothered him was that they no longer seemed surprising.

"Walk me through it."

The city expanded above the table.

Millions of tiny lights.

Millions of people.

Millions of lives.

"Imagine 100 south korean women and 100 south korean men."

"Okay."

"Replacement requires approximately two hundred children."

"Roughly."

"Instead they produce sixty."

The number appeared.

100 women.

60 children.

Elias winced.

Even seeing it that way felt wrong.

"Now divide by two."

Thirty future women.

The numbers appeared.

Then Opi continued.

"Those thirty future women produce now eighteen children."

The display changed again.

200 adults.

60 children.

18 grandchildren.

The kitchen became quiet.

"Jesus."

"That is approximately the usual human response."

Elias stared at the numbers.

Two generations.

The entire structure collapsed.

Not gradually.

Violently.

Mathematically.

Then Opi crushed every speck of optimism he had left.

“Unfortunately, this example is still optimistic.”

Elias looked up. “How?” “

It assumes the fertility rate remains constant at 0.6.” The graph changed. “

The TFR is not the only important demographic metric that is falling in south korea, the marriage rate is collapsing as well.

When the marriage rate continues falling, if children continue losing cultural desirability, then the more likely TFR curve is not flat - it's downward.

New numbers appeared.

2028: 0.6.

2031: 0.5.

2034: 0.4. "

2037: 0.3.

2040: 0.2.

2043: 0.1.

2046: 0.0.

Elias stared at the final number with a look of utter shock on his face.

“Zero ? “

Functionally zero,” Opi said. “Not biologically impossible.

But socially close enough that a large modern city stops producing children as a meaningful population process.”

The room went very still. “

So the two hundred men and women don’t just become sixty children and eighteen grandchildren.” “

No,” Opi said. “That is the pleasant version.” “

The graph extended forward until the line touched the bottom. “

If the downward trend continues, by 2046 the city has effectively exited reproduction.”

Elias sat back slowly. “

That’s eighteen years.” “

Yes.” “

One high school generation.”

“And after that?” Opi’s light dimmed. “

After that, the city is no longer aging. It is, in all ways that matter, already dead

The orb drifted toward the window.

"Humans imagine collapse incorrectly."

"How so?"

"They imagine empty cities."

"Riots."

"Violence."

"Burning buildings."

The city image rotated.

"Those things happen occasionally."

"What happens most often?"

Opi dimmed slightly.

"Nothing."

"Nothing?"

"The schools close."

"The classrooms empty."

"The sports leagues disappear."

"The maternity wards shut down."

"The neighborhoods become quiet."

"The average age rises."

"The future slowly leaves."

Elias looked out across his pasture.

For reasons he couldn't explain, that sounded worse.

"Then there is the labor problem."

The city image changed.

Construction sites.

Electrical substations.

Water treatment facilities.

Road crews.

Ports.

Power lines.

Garbage trucks.

"The jobs nobody thinks about."

Elias nodded.

"The jobs everybody notices when they stop happening."

"Exactly."

The orb brightened.

"Who repairs the sewer mains?"

"Who climbs power poles during winter storms?"

"Who pours concrete?"

"Who repairs roads?"

"Who crawls inside industrial equipment?"

She paused.

"Who empties the garbage?"

Every advanced civilization ultimately runs on the labor of healthy males."

Older men usually participate in training or supervisory roles but

almost all of the most dangerous, really unpleasant, physically demanding work is performed by YOUNG men specifically.

The answer came instantly.

Not ideological.

Not emotional.

Just observational.

Elias nodded.

That matched everything he had seen throughout his life.

The display shifted again.

This time showing age distributions.

The graph looked wrong.

Top-heavy.

Old.

Fragile.

"Here's the uncomfortable part."

"Oh good."

"The young do not merely support the old economically."

"They support them physically."

The orb rotated slowly.

"What happens when there aren't enough?"

Opi became very still.

"Eventually?"

"Yes."

"The society begins consuming itself."

Elias frowned.

"Explain."

"Road maintenance is deferred."

"Infrastructure replacement is delayed."

"Power grids become less reliable."

"Housing construction slows."

"Transportation costs rise."

The list continued.

None of it sounded dramatic.

That was the disturbing part.

It sounded normal.

Plausible.

Incremental.

"Every year becomes slightly worse than the previous year."

The city above the table seemed to dim.

"Until eventually the civilization can no longer maintain the complexity it inherited."

For several moments neither spoke.

Finally Elias asked the obvious question.

"If it's this bad, why aren't more people talking about it?"

Opi actually laughed.

A warm Velhari laugh.

"Because humans are extraordinarily good at ignoring exponential curves."

"That's unfair."

"It is mathematically accurate."

The graph reappeared.

The numbers looked absurd.

Impossible.

And yet they weren't.

That was the problem.

The math wasn't speculative.

The math had already begun.

After a long silence Elias rubbed his face.

"You're telling me this isn't a future problem."

"No."

The orb's lights softened.

"It is a present problem with a delayed consequence."

For several moments the only sound in the kitchen was the ticking of the clock.

Finally Elias looked up.

"Okay."

Opi brightened slightly.

"Okay?"

"Now I understand why you're worried."

The orb remained silent.

Then:

"I was hoping you would."

Oddly enough, Elias found himself feeling relieved.

Not happy.

Certainly not optimistic.

But relieved.

For the first time since Opi had appeared in his pasture, the problem had acquired a shape.

A target.

Something he could point at.

Something he could attack.

Most people looked at demographic collapse and saw politics. Or economics. Or culture. Or social media. Or housing prices. Or education.

Elias just saw math

And mathematics had one enormous advantage over politics.

It didn't care what anyone believed.

The graph hanging above the table wasn't ideological.

It wasn't partisan.

It wasn't even particularly complicated.

Any reasonably bright fourteen-year-old with a calculator could understand it.

A fertility rate below replacement eventually produced fewer children.

Fewer children eventually produced fewer adults.

Fewer adults eventually produced fewer families.

The process repeated until the civilization either reversed course...

...or disappeared.

The math was almost insultingly simple.

Which was probably why so many people ignored it.

Humans had a remarkable ability to dismiss consequences that arrived slowly.

A man would panic over a house fire.

Very few panicked over termites.

Even though the termites often did more damage.

He stared at the graph one last time.

Then smiled.

Opi immediately became suspicious.

"What?"

Elias pointed at the projection.

"That's what."

"The graph?"

"The target."

The orb rotated slightly.

"Explain."

"For twenty years I've listened to people describe a thousand different problems."

"Politics."

"Corruption."

"Debt."

"Housing."

"Education."

"Crime."

"Healthcare."

He shrugged.

"Most of them are either symptoms or arguments about symptoms."

Opi brightened slightly.

"Continue."

"But this?"

He pointed again.

"This is measurable."

"This is testable."

"This is objective."

The smile widened.

"And most importantly..."

The orb was already anticipating the answer.

"...it gives us a scorecard."

For several seconds neither spoke.

Then Opi laughed softly.

A warm Velhari laugh.

"You are viewing the possible extinction of your civilization as an engineering problem."

"Because it is an engineering problem."

"It is also a social problem."

"A psychological problem."

"A cultural problem."

"A biological problem."

Elias nodded.

"Sure."

"But eventually every one of those gets measured by the same number."

The graph remained suspended above the table.

Births.

Future births.

Future families.

Future civilization.

All collapsing into a single measurable outcome.

"You're not wrong," Opi admitted.

"Of course I'm not."

"You have known about this problem for approximately fifty-three minutes."

"What can I say ... I've had a really productive morning."

The orb brightened.

Elias stood and walked toward the kitchen window.

The pasture stretched away beneath the afternoon sun.

For a moment he found himself thinking about Noah.

And Claire.

And children not yet born.

Children who might never be born.

Entire family lines quietly ending because millions of individual decisions happened to point in the same direction.

The thought bothered him more than he expected.

Not because it was tragic.

Because it was unnecessary.

Eventually he turned back toward Opi.

"We can't force people."

"No."

"We can't manipulate them."

"No."

"We can't build breeding programs."

"Absolutely not."

"We can't use mind control."

The orb brightened.

"Humans become alarmingly interested in mind control once they discover it exists."

"I noticed."

"Your species has some concerning instincts."

Elias laughed.

He sat back down.

"But we don't need any of that."

The orb became very still.

This was the voice she had been waiting to hear.

The one that emerged when Elias stopped reacting and started solving.

"We don't?"

"No."

He leaned forward.

"If the fertility rate is the scorecard, then all we need to do is identify the conditions under which normal healthy people voluntarily choose to have children ."

and if possible, to have them in stable marriages. but there are options to do that in some optional ways if it truly becomes necessary.

Opi said nothing.

"If those conditions can be created..."

He paused.

"...then they can be tested."

The orb brightened.

"Proof of concept."

"Exactly."

"Then scale."

"Exactly."

"Then iterate."

"Now you're getting it."

For the first time all day, Opi looked genuinely pleased.

Not because he had produced an answer.

The Velhari never trusted answers.

Because he had finally asked the correct question.

Not: How do we force people to have children?

Not: How do we manipulate society?

Not: How do we cheat?

Instead: What conditions cause people to willingly build families?

That question was allowed.

The Hand would permit it.

Velhari law would permit it.

Human dignity would permit it.

Most importantly, it was a question reality could answer.

Given enough experiments.

Given enough failures.

Given enough time.

Then another realization arrived.

One that simplified the problem even further.

Most people, when confronted with a civilizational crisis, immediately started talking about politics.

Elias had spent enough years around governments, corporations, and large institutions to know better.

Politics was where solutions went to die.

The people involved were not necessarily stupid. Many were intelligent. Some were genuinely trying to help.

That wasn't the problem.

The problem was scale.

The larger an institution became, the more if morphed into bureaucratic fiefdoms consumed with incessant turf wars.

The United States government was not going to reinvent itself overnight.

The universities were not going to reinvent themselves overnight.

And The bureaucracy certainly wasn't going to reinvent itself overnight or more probably, ever.

Even if everyone suddenly agreed there was a problem—which they didn't—the machinery simply moved too slowly.

Elias had seen software projects fail exactly this way.

Everybody knew the system was broken.

Nobody could stop using it long enough to replace it.

Eventually you stopped trying.

You built something new beside it.

That realization hit him so hard he actually sat up in his chair.

Opi noticed immediately.

"You have a look."

"What look?"

"The one that usually precedes trouble."

Elias smiled.

"I finally know what we're building."

The orb drifted closer.

"Tell me."

"A parallel society."

For several seconds Opi remained silent.

Then her lights brightened.

"Interesting."

"We don't fix the existing system."

"No?"

"No."

Elias shook his head.

"Impossible."

He pointed toward the demographic projections.

"We don't have fifty years."

"We probably don't have twenty."

"Maybe not even ten."

The orb didn't disagree.

That worried him slightly.

"We build something completely new alongside the existing society."

"Voluntary."

"Functional."

"Attractive enough that people choose it."

The orb brightened further.

"A competing society."

"Exactly."

For the first time all afternoon Opi looked genuinely pleased.

"That is a very Velhari solution."

"Good."

"Most species attempt reform."

"And?"

"Most fail."

The orb rotated slowly.

"The successful ones usually create alternatives."

Elias laughed.

"Good. Because reform sounds exhausting."

For several moments he stared at the map of Washington still hanging above the table.

Then another realization arrived.

This one really made him smile.

A dangerous smile.

The sort that usually appeared shortly before somebody regretted something.

Opi noticed immediately.

"You have another one of those looks."

"Yeah."

"What kind this time?"

"The kind where I just figured out where to find my people."

The orb brightened.

"Your people?"

"The first recruits."

For several moments Opi simply watched him.

Then:

"Who?"

Elias laughed.

"Not who."

"Where."

He walked over to the map.

Seattle glowed softly.

A city overflowing with money, technology, politics, bureaucracy, activism, good intentions, bad incentives, and enough dysfunction to fill a milion garbage trucks.

Most people looked at those failures and saw hopelessness.

Elias saw something else.

Selection.

"Think about it."

The orb became very still.

That was Velhari for listening.

"Who survives four years homeless?"

Elias asked.

"Who survives addiction?"

"Who survives abandonment?"

"Who survives losing everything?"

The city hung silently above the table.

"They aren't weak."

"No."

"They aren't fragile."

"No."

"They've already endured things most comfortable people would collapse under."

The orb brightened.

"Continue."

Elias smiled.

"Everybody keeps looking at them and seeing broken people."

"What do you see?"

He looked directly at her.

"I see survivors."

The kitchen became quiet.

Because they both understood the implication.

A parallel civilization would require builders.

Risk-takers.

Believers.

People willing to try something completely insane.

People with very little left to lose.

And perhaps more importantly...

People capable of recognizing hope when it appeared.

Finally Elias folded his arms.

"All right, Ope."

The orb brightened.

"Yes?"

"Let's go find out what actually works."
