Chapter 8: Nizhoni Nez

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 8 of 32

← Chapter 7Part A IndexChapter 9 →

Nizhoni Nez sat beneath the overpass and tried not to move.

Moving hurt.

Breathing hurt.

Existing hurt.

The left side of her face throbbed.

Her ribs hurt.

Her shoulder hurt.

Even her jaw hurt.

She touched the bruise beneath her eye and immediately regretted it.

The pain was sharp enough to make her flinch.

Only six hours earlier she had been lying on the stained carpet of a motel room staring at the ceiling.

Blood in her mouth.

Tears in her eyes.

Trying very hard not to make a sound.

Her pimp stood over her screaming.

Not enough money.

Never enough money.

The words barely mattered anymore.

There was always a reason.

Always a problem.

Always a punishment.

The first punch had knocked her into the wall.

The second had put her on the floor.

After that she mostly remembered the shoes.

The shouting.

The threats.

Then eventually silence.

The memory made her stomach twist.

Nizhoni pulled her knees tighter against her chest.

That hurt too.

Everything hurt.

She was seventeen years old.

Hungry.

Dirty.

Cold.

Tired.

So unbelievably tired.

The foil packet in her pocket felt like the only friend she had left.

A little fentanyl.

A little escape.

No pain.

No fear.

No memories.

She closed her eyes.

Immediately she was eight years old again.

"Pancakes."

The smell hit her first.

Butter.

real Maple syrup.

The faint scent of coffee.

Her grandmother stood at the stove wearing her faded blue apron.

The old woman smiled without turning around.

"Sit down."

Nizhoni climbed into her chair.

The kitchen felt enormous.

The old house creaked around them.

Safe.

Warm.

Home.

Her grandmother placed a pancake on the plate.

It was enormous.

Far too big.

Nizhoni laughed.

"Grandma."

"What?"

"It's bigger than the plate."

"I know."

"You did it again."

"I absolutely did."

Nizhoni remembered laughing.

Really laughing.

The kind that came from deep inside.

The memory hurt worse than the bruises.

She opened her eyes.

The overpass returned.

Concrete.

Trash.

Rainwater.

The smell of urine.

No pancakes.

No grandmother.

No home.

Her mother had abandoned her long ago.

Her grandmother had stepped into the gap.

Then cancer came.

After that everything fell apart.

Foster homes.

Running away.

The streets.

The drugs.

The men.

Nizhoni stared at the traffic moving overhead.

When she was little she used to play with dolls for hours.

Most girls dreamed about becoming princesses.

Nizhoni dreamed about having a family.

A mother.

A father.

Brothers.

Sisters.

A house filled with noise and laughter.

A place where people loved each other and stayed.

The father doll always came home.

The mother doll always smiled.

Nobody hit anyone.

Nobody disappeared.

Nobody left.

She remembered believing that someday it would be real.

Now the idea seemed absurd.

Like magic.

Like fairy tales.

Nizhoni pulled the foil packet from her pocket and stared at it.

The world had finally convinced her of something.

She wasn't getting the family.

She wasn't getting the husband.

She wasn't getting the children.

She wasn't getting the future.

That dream belonged to somebody else.

Not her.

A tear slipped down her cheek.

Then another.

The realization settled over her with crushing certainty.

She was never getting out.

Never.

The thought should have terrified her.

Instead it just made her tired.

So very tired.

Above her, traffic rolled through the night.

Nobody noticed the girl beneath the bridge.

Nobody knew she existed.

Nobody knew how close she was to giving up.

The last thing she thought about was pancakes.
