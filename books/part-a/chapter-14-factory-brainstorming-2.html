Chapter 14: Factory Brainstorming 2

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 14 of 32

← Chapter 13Part A IndexChapter 15 →

Elias stared at the charts for nearly a minute.

Then he swore.

Not loudly.

Just the sort of profanity a man uses when reality has once again become inconvenient.

"What now?" Opi asked.

"I think we may be solving the wrong half of the equation."

The orb brightened.

"Explain."

Elias pointed at one of the graphs.

"Male earnings."

"Yes."

"Show me childlessness by income percentile."

The graph immediately changed.

The pattern was obvious.

Brutally obvious.

At the lowest income levels, childlessness climbed dramatically.

At the highest income levels, it dropped.

The curve looked almost like gravity.

Everything flowing downhill.

Elias shook his head.

"That is insane."

"Not particularly."

"It is if you're trying to save a civilization."

Opi floated closer.

"The relationship is well documented."

Elias continued studying the graph.

"The poorest men almost never have children."

"Relative to the highest earners, significantly less often."

"And the richest men have them constantly."

"More frequently."

The chart vanished.

A new one appeared.

"This becomes even more apparent when viewed longitudinally."

Elias frowned.

"What am I looking at?"

"Wage growth."

Several colored lines stretched across the display.

"United States data."

"Actual outcomes?"

"Actual outcomes."

The orb highlighted one line.

"For every approximately four percent increase in a man's earnings, the probability of fatherhood rises by roughly one point one percent."

Elias blinked.

"That's a huge effect."

"Yes."

Another line appeared.

This one moved in the opposite direction.

"And women?"

"For every approximately four percent increase in earnings, the probability of motherhood decreases by roughly one point three percent."

Elias stared.

Then stared some more.

Then sat back.

"Well."

"Yes."

"That is awkward."

"A statistically common reaction."

For several seconds neither spoke.

Finally Elias pointed at the graph.

"So men and women are responding differently."

"Correct."

"Very differently."

"Correct."

He thought about it.

"If you're a man."

"Yes."

"Higher income increases your likelihood of becoming a father."

"Generally."

"If you're a woman."

"Higher income decreases your likelihood of becoming a mother."

"Generally."

The graphs vanished.

The silence lingered.

Then Elias asked the obvious question.

"Why?"

Opi's lights flickered.

"That question has generated approximately fourteen thousand published hypotheses."

"Only fourteen thousand?"

"I am rounding down."

Elias laughed.

Then became serious again.

"What does your data say?"

The orb became quiet.

"The answer appears multifactorial."

"Meaning complicated."

"Meaning humans enjoy turning every issue into seventeen issues."

The orb projected several overlapping networks.

Career timing.

Marriage timing.

Educational attainment.

Partner selection.

Housing.

Childcare.

Cultural expectations.

Economic pressures.

Everything connected to everything else.

Opi highlighted one section.

"However, there is one observation that appears repeatedly."

Elias leaned forward.

"What?"

"Women generally do not reduce their partner standards as their own success increases."

"Interesting."

"In many cases the opposite occurs."

Elias rubbed his chin.

"So successful women often seek equally successful men."

"Frequently."

"And successful men can form relationships across a wider range of income levels."

"Frequently."

Elias sat silently.

Then suddenly pointed.

"Wait."

"What?"

"That creates a bottleneck."

Opi brightened.

"Continue."

"If a woman earning two hundred thousand dollars wants a man earning two hundred thousand dollars."

"Usually more."

"And there aren't enough of those men."

"Potentially."

"Then a portion of those women remain unmarried."

"One possible outcome."

"And the men?"

"The men may have substantially more options."

Elias leaned back.

"That doesn't sound like a fertility problem."

Opi tilted slightly.

"No?"

"It sounds like a matching problem."

For a moment the orb said nothing.

Then:

"Interesting."

Elias stood up and began pacing again.

"No seriously."

He pointed toward the valley.

"What if we're thinking about this backwards?"

"Explain."

"We keep talking about babies."

"Yes."

"We keep talking about fertility."

"Yes."

"We keep talking about attraction."

"Yes."

"But none of those are the bottleneck."

The orb brightened.

"What is?"

Elias stopped walking.

"Pair formation."

The words hung in the air.

"Everything starts there."

He pointed toward Opi.

"If nobody forms stable pairs."

"No families."

"No children."

"No civilization."

Opi became completely still.

A habit she had whenever something truly interested her.

Elias continued.

"Maybe the problem isn't fertility."

"Maybe the problem is that modern society has become terrible at helping people find compatible partners."

The orb remained silent.

"Maybe we've spent decades optimizing everything except the thing that matters."

"Which is?"

"Helping men and women build families."

The valley became quiet.

Wind moved through the pine trees.

Somewhere below, construction crews were finishing another cluster of homes for the Parallel.

People who yesterday had been homeless.

People who finally had stability.

People who finally had a future.

And suddenly Elias understood something.

Housing was not the destination.

Recovery was not the destination.

Employment was not the destination.

Those were all foundations.

Necessary foundations.

But foundations nonetheless.

The real objective was what came after.

A society where people actually wanted to build a future together.

He looked up at Opi.

"Tell me something."

"What?"

"If we doubled every man's income tomorrow."

The orb immediately answered.

"Birth rates would increase."

"By how much?"

"Not enough."

Elias smiled.

"Exactly."

The answer wasn't money.

The answer wasn't housing.

The answer wasn't healthcare.

Those things mattered.

But none of them appeared to be the deepest layer.

The deepest layer remained stubbornly human.

Men.

Women.

Attraction.

Trust.

Commitment.

Family.

The same puzzle every civilization had wrestled with since the beginning of history.

And somehow modern civilization appeared to be getting worse at solving it.

Which meant, Elias realized with growing concern, that the next phase of the Parallel might be far more difficult than anything they had attempted so far.
