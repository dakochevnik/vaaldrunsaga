Chapter 15: Proof is in the Pudding

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 15 of 32

← Chapter 14Part A IndexChapter 16 →

The humor disappeared first.

Not completely.

But enough that Opi noticed.

Elias pushed his empty plate away.

The conversation had gone from impossible to absurd to fascinating to terrifying in less than an hour.

Alien civilizations.

The Culling.

Humanity's future.

VaalDruns.

The possibility that the entire direction of his life was about to change.

Yet suddenly none of that seemed like the most important thing.

A different thought had arrived.

One that immediately pushed everything else aside.

He looked directly at Opi.

"Before we go any further."

The orb became still.

A very Velhari stillness.

Attentive.

Listening.

"Before I agree to anything."

"All right."

"There is one condition."

The lights inside the orb shifted softly.

"I expected there would be."

Elias nodded.

"If what you're saying is true..."

He paused.

"If I become involved in this..."

Another pause.

"If I become your VaalDrun..."

The word still felt strange.

"...eventually somebody is going to hate what we're doing."

"An excellent prediction."

"Somebody's going to oppose it."

"Also likely."

"Somebody may try to hurt me."

"Almost guaranteed."

"Fine."

He shrugged.

"I can live with that."

Then his expression hardened.

"But Noah and Claire are off limits."

Silence.

"I won't drag them into this."

The orb said nothing.

"I won't put them in danger."

Still nothing.

"I won't accept a future where saving humanity costs me my children."

The kitchen became completely quiet.

Finally Opi spoke.

"You believe I might ask that of you."

Elias looked at her.

"No."

The answer surprised her.

"I believe reality might."

The orb became very still.

Because that was a better answer.

A wiser answer.

Finally she said:

"You are requesting protection."

"No."

Elias shook his head.

"Protection is the wrong word."

"Explain."

He leaned back.

"I don't want Noah and Claire wrapped in bubble wrap."

The orb brightened.

"An intriguing human safety technology."

"I want them free."

"Good."

"I want them living their own lives."

"Better."

"I want them making mistakes."

"Necessary."

"I want them falling in love."

The lights inside the orb softened.

"I want them deciding who they become."

He pointed at her.

"And I need to know that whatever insanity we're about to unleash on the world doesn't interfere with that."

The orb remained silent for several seconds.

Then:

"Would you like a demonstration?"

Elias immediately frowned.

That never led anywhere normal.

"What kind of demonstration?"

"A father-shaped demonstration."

"That is not reassuring."

The orb floated upward.

Without warning the air above the kitchen table rippled.

Not dramatically.

Not like science fiction.

More like reality had briefly forgotten what shape it was supposed to be.

A shimmering window opened.

Elias froze.

The image sharpened.

Austin.

A small apartment.

Noah.

His son sat at a kitchen table wearing a faded Tesla shirt and eating cereal directly from the box.

Elias smiled despite himself.

"Some things never change."

"Observe."

The image expanded.

Not forward in time.

Outward.

The apartment.

The building.

The parking lot.

The street.

Vehicles.

People.

Animals.

Infrastructure.

Movement.

Patterns.

The amount of information was overwhelming.

"What am I looking at?"

"My current protective model."

Elias frowned.

"What does that mean?"

"It means that at this exact moment there are approximately one thousand four hundred and twelve plausible pathways by which Noah Mercer could suffer serious harm in the next twelve months."

Elias nearly choked.

"I'm sorry, what?"

"One thousand four hundred and twelve."

"That is not a comforting number."

"It is actually exceptionally low."

"You're making that up."

"I am not."

"You absolutely are."

"One thousand four hundred and thirteen."

Elias blinked.

"What changed?"

"You stood up."

For a moment he just stared at her.

Then pointed.

"You're enjoying this."

"I am."

The image changed.

A truck.

An intersection.

A distracted driver.

Gone.

A workplace accident.

Gone.

A medical emergency.

Gone.

Another.

Gone.

Another.

Gone.

Dozens.

Hundreds.

Thousands.

Each one disappearing before fully developing.

Like branches being trimmed from a tree.

"What are you doing?"

"Demonstrating."

"Demonstrating what?"

"The difference between observation and intervention."

The images vanished.

The kitchen returned.

"Under Velhari law I may not control Noah's life."

"Good."

"I may not dictate his choices."

"Good."

"I may not imprison him in safety."

"Very good."

The orb drifted closer.

"But if you become my VaalDrun..."

The lights warmed.

"...I can ensure that random chance becomes dramatically less hostile."

Elias stared.

"How dramatically?"

Opi paused.

"Would you like the statistical answer or the father answer?"

"The father answer."

For the first time all morning her voice became almost playful.

"The father answer is that if Noah slips on a wet floor, he will still fall."

"Okay."

"But if a drunk driver runs a red light at ninety miles per hour, reality may begin experiencing a remarkable series of coincidences."

Elias barked out a laugh.

"Coincidences?"

"Extraordinary coincidences."

"Such as?"

"The driver discovering a sudden need to stop for coffee."

"A tire puncture."

"A traffic signal malfunction."

"A police officer becoming curious."

"A thousand tiny possibilities."

She paused.

"All completely natural."

"Of course."

"Entirely."

Elias rubbed his face.

"You can actually do that?"

"I can do considerably more than that."

Then her tone softened.

"But I understand the distinction you are making."

The humor disappeared.

"Your children are not treasures because they are valuable."

Elias looked up.

"They are treasures because they are yours."

The kitchen became quiet.

"Yes."

The answer came softly.

"Yes."

The orb glowed warmly.

"I selected you because you think that way."

Another pause.

Then:

"And because Noah and Claire are already among the humans I would least like to see harmed."

That surprised him.

"Why?"

The answer arrived instantly.

"Because they are the children of my preferred human."

Elias stared for a moment.

Then laughed.

"Opi."

"Yes?"

"That may be the most female answer you've given all morning."

The orb brightened smugly.

"I have had nine thousand one hundred and sixty-eight years to practice."

"You're impossible."

"I have received that feedback before."

"How many times?"

The orb paused.

"Approximately three million."

"That many?"

"I rounded down."

Elias laughed again.

For the first time since the conversation had turned serious, the knot in his chest loosened.

Not completely.

But enough.

Elias folded his arms.

"You're telling me the universe punishes civilizations that cheat."

"I am telling you that every civilization that attempted to replace individual choice with centralized control eventually entered its own Culling."

She paused.

"The mechanism remains unknown."

"That's unsettling."

"Extremely."

For several moments Elias considered this.

Then a grin slowly appeared.

"Hold on."

The orb brightened suspiciously.

"What?"

"You've modeled all this."

"Yes."

"You've looked at every possible solution."

"Many."

"And one of them was probably..."

Elias paused dramatically.

The orb already knew where this was going.

"No."

"...keep the Amish."

"No."

"Just the Amish."

No

"They seem to be doing fine."

"Elias."

"We just need to save the Amish."

No. No No and No

"And Everybody else gets a participation trophy and a nice thank-you card."

The orb's lights flickered.

Which he was beginning to recognize as suppressed laughter.

"You are proposing genocide."

"I'm proposing a very selective admissions process."

The orb became silent for a moment.

Then:

"You would make a terrible QI."

"Thank you."

The humor faded.

Opi drifted closer.

"There is another reason."

"What?"

"Killing is inefficient."

That got his attention.

"Explain."

"If I wished to stop an individual from causing harm..."

The orb brightened.

"...why would I kill them?"

Elias frowned.

"Because that's usually how humans solve that problem."

"Yes."

Her tone suggested this remained one of humanity's less impressive traits.

"But death permanently removes information."

"Okay."

"It removes potential."

"It removes future choices."

She paused.

"It is wasteful."

Elias stared.

"What would you do instead?"

The orb's lights brightened.

"I would temporarily disable them."

"Disable?"

"Yes."

"Like a computer?"

"A biological computer."

The way she said it was so casual it took him a moment to process.

"You can do that?"

"Of course."

"How?"

The orb rotated.

"Imagine a very brief interruption to conscious neural activity."

"You mean unconscious."

"I mean unconscious."

"For how long?"

"Five minutes."

"An hour."

"A week."

"Whatever is necessary."

Elias blinked.

"You can literally turn people off."

"Temporarily."

"That's insane."

"It is considerably less insane than killing them."

The kitchen fell silent again.

Finally Elias asked:

"So if we can't force people..."

"Correct."

"And we can't engineer humanity into compliance..."

"Correct."

"And we can't solve everything by acting like gods..."

"Correct."

"Then what exactly are we supposed to do?"

The orb's lights softened.

For the first time all morning her answer came immediately.

"We remind people why civilization is worth continuing."

Elias sat quietly.

Outside, nothing looked different.

The trees.

The pasture.

The morning sun.

Everything appeared perfectly normal.

Which was the problem.

Most civilizations probably looked normal right before they started disappearing.

He looked back at Opi.

"You know," he said, "when I woke up this morning I thought I was going for a run."

"Yes."

"That feels like a very long time ago."

Opi brightened.

"You have adapted unusually well."

Elias laughed.

"Either that or I've completely lost my mind."

"Those outcomes are not mutually exclusive."

For several seconds they simply looked at one another.

Then both started laughing.
