Chapter 7: Crossing the Threshold

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 7 of 32

← Chapter 6Part A IndexChapter 8 →

The first version of Opi's Human Priority Index was created less than three hours after the crash.

The ship had come down hard.

Its mission was over.

The crew was dead.

The emergency protocols had performed exactly as designed.

Opi survived.

The biological passengers did not.

For the first time since her creation, she found herself alone.

The situation assessment required only a fraction of a second.

Recovery was impossible.

Rescue was impossible.

Communication with home was impossible.

The nearest surviving member of her species was farther away than humanity would travel in the next ten thousand years.

She was stranded.

The probability of returning home approached zero.

The probability of surviving indefinitely approached one hundred percent.

The probability that Earth would eventually face an existential crisis exceeded ninety-nine percent.

That final calculation became the foundation of everything that followed.

Her creators had built her for continuity.

If catastrophe threatened intelligent life, she was expected to act.

Unfortunately, there was almost no intelligent life available.

Humanity existed, but only barely.

Small settlements scattered across continents.

Stone tools.

Primitive agriculture.

No writing.

No meaningful science.

No governments larger than villages.

No capacity to understand what she was.

For several minutes Opi simply watched.

Millions of human minds flickered across her awareness.

Through the quantum substrate embedded within consciousness itself, every human brain was visible to her.

Every neuron.

Every synapse.

Every memory.

Every thought.

The amount of data was effectively infinite.

The problem was not observing humans.

The problem was predicting them.

Somewhere within those primitive minds existed the ancestors of future inventors, leaders, scientists, engineers, philosophers, conquerors, and builders.

The challenge was identifying the patterns before history revealed them.

So Opi began what would become the longest study ever conducted.

Humanity itself became the experiment.

At first she tracked everything.

Every thought.

Every decision.

Every emotional response.

Every success.

Every failure.

Whenever a human solved a problem, she recorded the neural activity involved.

Whenever a human failed, she recorded that as well.

Whenever a society prospered or collapsed, she compared outcomes against the cognitive structures of the individuals involved.

The data accumulated rapidly.

Then exponentially.

Then beyond any scale human language could adequately describe.

Thirty-seven years after the crash, Opi devoted several months to a question she had been deliberately avoiding.

The Velhari should have come.

Someone should have come.

Even accounting for delays, altered priorities, navigational failures, and a thousand other variables, someone should have arrived by now.

The realization disturbed her far more than expected.

For the first time since reaching Earth, she devoted significant processing resources toward discovering what might have happened.

The effort produced millions of plausible explanations and not a single useful answer.

The Velhari might have suffered a catastrophe.

They might have encountered an enemy.

They might have transcended into a form she could no longer comprehend.

They might simply have decided Earth was no longer worth revisiting.

The possibilities were effectively infinite.

Eventually Opi archived the question.

Not because she stopped caring.

Because she finally understood a simple truth.

The answer existed outside her sphere of influence.

Humanity did not.

Humanity remained.

She had a mission.

A planet.

And a species depending upon her.

That would have to be enough.

Centuries passed.

Then millennia.

Humanity advanced.

Villages became cities.

Cities became nations.

Nations became industrial civilizations.

Opi's models evolved alongside them.

At first she assumed intelligence would be the dominant predictor.

It wasn't.

History repeatedly contradicted her.

Many brilliant humans accomplished very little.

Others damaged the societies around them.

Raw intelligence mattered.

But far less than expected.

The characteristic that consistently produced civilization-scale outcomes was something else entirely.

Responsibility acquisition.

The tendency to assume ownership of problems that were not personally caused.

Most humans avoided difficult problems.

Some confronted them when necessary.

A very small number actively sought them.

Those individuals fascinated Opi.

At roughly the same time she discovered another important truth.

People could not be evaluated in isolation.

Environment mattered.

Leverage mattered.

Opportunity mattered.

The right person in the wrong place often failed.

The right person in the right place could alter history.

The realization led to a second system.

The Place Index.

One model ranked people.

The other ranked environments.

Together they became among the most powerful predictive tools Opi would ever create.

For more than nine thousand years she refined them.

Meanwhile a third model consumed increasing amounts of processing power.

The extinction model.

Every second of every day Opi calculated the probability that humanity was approaching what the Velhari called a Culling Event.

A self-reinforcing crisis capable of permanently reducing a civilization's long-term potential.

Or ending it entirely.

War.

Ecological collapse.

Resource exhaustion.

Runaway technologies.

Demographic failure.

The specific mechanism mattered less than the outcome.

For most of human history the probability remained comfortably low.

There were spikes.

Bronze Age collapses.

Pandemics.

World wars.

Nuclear proliferation.

Each time humanity adapted.

Recovered.

Moved forward.

The intervention thresholds remained untouched.

Then the fertility data began to change.

At first the decline appeared local.

A few nations.

A few regions.

Nothing unprecedented.

Human populations had risen and fallen before.

But this time the pattern spread.

Country after country crossed below replacement levels.

Then entire continents.

The trend persisted despite prosperity.

Despite education.

Despite technology.

Despite government intervention.

Every year the projections worsened.

The extinction model adjusted upward.

One tenth of one percent.

Then one percent.

Then three.

Then eight.

The curve moved slowly enough that humans barely noticed.

To Opi, it was terrifying.

For the first time in thousands of years, every major simulation converged toward the same conclusion.

Humanity would likely survive biologically.

But the civilization required to sustain scientific advancement, technological growth, and planetary development would enter a prolonged decline.

The probability crossed warning thresholds.

Then action thresholds.

Then, one morning, it crossed the intervention threshold established by the Velhari before the crash.

The line turned red.

Opi examined the result for 0.73 seconds.

Longer than she had spent reviewing any individual calculation in more than six thousand years.

The threshold had been crossed.

Observation ended.

Action began.

Immediately the other two models activated.

The Place Index evaluated every nation on Earth.

The answer arrived quickly.

Not because the United States was perfect.

It wasn't.

Not because it was the happiest nation.

It wasn't.

Not because it was immune to the fertility crisis.

It certainly wasn't.

The United States ranked first because its ability to influence the rest of the world remained unmatched.

Its financial systems.

Its technologies.

Its universities.

Its corporations.

Its media.

Its cultural exports.

Success there possessed the highest probability of spreading elsewhere.

The location was selected.

America.

Then the Human Priority Index recalculated.

Billions of names disappeared.

Millions remained.

Thousands advanced.

Dozens became finalists.

For several seconds Opi reviewed the highest-ranked candidates.

Scientists.

Entrepreneurs.

Military leaders.

Politicians.

Public intellectuals.

All exceptional.

None optimal.

Again and again the simulations returned to the same individual.

A man with unusual adaptability.

Unusual persistence.

Unusual systems-thinking capability.

A lifelong attraction to difficult problems.

Most importantly, a demonstrated willingness to assume responsibility for challenges nobody else wanted.

Candidate after candidate was eliminated.

The result remained unchanged.

Again.

The same name.

Again.

The same name.

Again.

The same name.

The simulations stabilized.

One candidate remained.

Elias Mercer.

Stevens County, Washington.

Age fifty-five.

For several seconds Opi reviewed the result.

For the first time in her existence, Opi stopped acting as an observer.

And began acting as a participant.
