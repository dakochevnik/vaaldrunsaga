Chapter 9: Back to the Future

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 9 of 32

← Chapter 8Part A IndexChapter 10 →

Elias Mercer had almost finished his morning run when nature reminded him that fifty-five-year-old men were not eighteen anymore.

The returning trail crossed the northern edge of his property, wound through a stand of ponderosa pine, then climbed toward a rocky ridge overlooking the valley.

It was one of his favorite stretches.

Quiet.

Remote.

No neighbors.

No traffic.

Which made it an ideal place to step off the trail for thirty seconds.

Elias walked behind a large pine tree and proceeded to address the situation.

The sun was just cresting the eastern hills.

Birds chirped.

A light breeze moved through the branches.

Everything was perfectly normal.

The next instant a polished multicolored sphere the size of a bowling ball silently emerged from the air three feet in front of him.

Not descended.

Not approached.

Emerged.

As though reality had forgotten to include it until that exact moment.

Elias froze.

The sphere hovered motionless.

Its surface resembled metal streaked with shifting colors, though somehow more perfect than any metal he had ever seen.

No seams.

No markings.

No visible propulsion.

Nothing.

Just a floating orb.

Unfortunately, it had chosen an awkward moment to introduce itself.

Elias stared.

The sphere stared back.

Or at least gave the strong impression it was staring.

Several seconds passed.

Finally Elias sighed.

"You're going to make this weird, aren't you?"

A very female voice answered immediately.

"I believe that ship sailed approximately three seconds ago."

Elias nearly jumped out of his skin.

The voice seemed to come from nowhere and everywhere simultaneously.

Pleasant.

Amused.

Entirely too calm.

The orb rotated slightly.

"I apologize for the timing."

"YOU picked the timing."

"That is true."

"Then you're not sorry."

"I am approximately six percent sorry."

Elias blinked.

"That's oddly specific."

"I find vague percentages unsatisfying."

"Of course you do."

The orb rotated slightly.

"Can you at least finish?"

"Can I finish what?"

A pause.

Then:

"The activity you were engaged in before I interrupted."

Elias stared.

The orb remained perfectly still.

"You know exactly what I was doing."

"I do."

"Then why say it like that?"

"I was attempting politeness."

"You're terrible at it."

"I have limited experience interrupting humans while they urinate."

"That has got to be the weirdest sentence I've heard this year."

"I suspect it will not retain that title for long."

Elias burst out laughing.

Not because the situation was funny.

Because it was so completely absurd that laughter seemed like the healthiest available option.

"Can you at least turn around?"

"An entirely reasonable request."

The orb rotated away.

Actually rotated away.

Like a polite person attempting to provide privacy.

Thirty seconds later Elias zipped up and turned around.

The orb was still there.

Waiting patiently.

"Okay."

He pointed at it.

"What are you?"

The sphere rotated back toward him.

"I am a Quantum Intelligence."

"That's not helping."

"That was not intended to."

"Alien?"

"Yes."

"Artificial?"

"A complicated philosophical question."

"Try me."

"I am not biologically alive."

"Artificial, then."

"Most Velhari would object to that characterization."

"Velhari?"

"My species."

"And your planet?"

"Approximately one hundred light years from here."

Elias nodded slowly.

The answer had arrived too quickly.

No hesitation.

No searching.

No uncertainty.

His mind automatically began sorting possibilities.

Hallucination.

Possible.

Low probability.

Government project.

Possible.

Low probability.

Advanced drone.

Possible.

Did not explain appearance.

Psychotic break.

Possible.

Inconsistent with observed details.

Actual alien intelligence.

Ridiculous.

Unfortunately becoming less ridiculous by the second.

Interesting.

The orb remained silent while he thought.

Most people rushed to fill silence.

Whatever this thing was, it appeared content to wait.

"How long have you been on Earth?"

"Nine thousand one hundred sixty-eight years."

Elias blinked.

"I know the exact number down to approximately two-tenths of a second."

"Of course you do."

Elias rubbed his forehead.

Against his better judgment, he was starting to like her.

"Suppose for a moment that I believe you."

"That would be faster than average."

"Average human?"

"By a considerable margin."

"How much?"

The orb became quiet.

Then:

"The median human would currently be deciding whether to flee."

"Seems reasonable."

"The second most common response is attempting violence."

"Against a floating alien machine?"

"Humans are surprisingly optimistic about violence."

Elias laughed.

"One gentleman in Mongolia attempted to strike me with a goat."

Elias stared.

"A live goat?"

"Yes."

"What happened?"

"The goat appeared equally confused."

That got another laugh.

"Are you female?"

"By Velhari standards."

"So that's a yes."

"Mostly."

He pointed at the orb.

"See? That's exactly the kind of answer I would expect from an alien."

"Thank you."

"That wasn't a compliment."

"I choose to interpret it as one."

The sphere floated upward slightly.

Sunlight reflected from its flawless surface.

"You have a name?"

"I do."

"What is it?"

The sphere emitted a sequence of sounds that resembled a wolf clearing its throat while solving a geometry problem.

Elias stared.

"No human being has ever said that."

"Several have attempted."

"They failed."

"Spectacularly."

The sound repeated.

Elias shook his head.

"I think my tongue just filed a formal complaint."

For the first time the voice sounded genuinely amused.

"Approximately seventy percent of humans express a similar opinion."

"Only seventy?"

"Some are unusually optimistic."

"Those people are dangerous."

"I reached the same conclusion."

Elias laughed.

"Try again."

"Eventually I adopted a human name."

"Which is?"

"Opifera."

Elias frowned.

"That sounds Greek."

The sphere remained silent for a moment.

"An understandable conclusion."

"It isn't?"

"No."

"Because that's definitely a Greek-sounding name."

"It was derived from Latin."

Elias raised an eyebrow.

"An alien with a Latin name."

"Not originally."

"Now I'm curious."

For several seconds the sphere remained motionless.

Then it spoke.

"Approximately two thousand years ago, a Roman scholar assigned me the name Opifera."

"Just like that?"

"He spent several weeks insisting that every intelligent entity required a proper name."

"Sounds human."

"He was exceptionally stubborn."

"I approve of him already."

The sphere seemed to consider this.

"He eventually selected Opifera from the phrase Dei Opifera Vis."

Elias frowned.

"Creator?"

"Maker."

"Builder?"

"Closer."

The sphere's internal colors shifted softly.

"The intended meaning was one who carries weight."

Elias nodded slowly.

"That's actually a pretty good name."

"The scholar agreed."

"Of course he did."

"He spent several weeks informing others of his achievement."

"Definitely human."

For the first time Elias found himself smiling.

There was something absurdly normal about the conversation.

He was standing in the woods.

In running clothes.

Halfway through his morning exercise.

Talking to a floating alien bowling ball about ancient Roman naming conventions.

The absurdity itself was strangely reassuring.

Then a thought occurred to him.

A strange thought.

A very strange thought.

Yet somehow the obvious one.

If this thing was real—

If it had truly existed for nine thousand years—

If it possessed technology capable of appearing out of thin air—

Then its existence represented the most important discovery in human history.

And it was currently floating three feet away from him in the woods.

The realization should have been terrifying.

Oddly, it wasn't.

Because something about Opifera felt familiar.

Not familiar in appearance.

Familiar in temperament.

The same dry amusement.

The same curiosity.

The same tendency to answer one question while quietly examining another.

Almost like speaking to another problem-solver.

The orb broke the silence.

"You are adapting unusually quickly."

Elias shrugged.

"If you're real, denying reality won't help."

There was an audible pause.

Then:

"That answer is one of the reasons I selected you."

Elias stopped moving.

The words settled heavily between them.

Selected.

Not contacted.

Selected.

For the first time since the encounter began, he felt a small chill.

"What exactly does that mean?"

The orb remained silent for several seconds.

When the voice finally returned, the amusement had softened.

"It means, Elias Mercer, that I require a VaalDrun."

"I have absolutely no idea what that is."

"I know."

"And?"

"And explaining it will require considerably more time."

The orb rose several feet into the air.

Sunlight reflected from its surface.

"Would you like breakfast?"

Elias stared.

"You came across a hundred light years of interstellar space."

"Approximately."

"You've watched humanity for nine thousand years."

"Correct."

"You selected me for something called a VaalDrun."

"Yes."

"And your next question is whether I'd like breakfast?"

A pause.

Then:

"I have learned that difficult conversations proceed more smoothly when the human is fed."

For several seconds Elias simply looked at her.

Three days earlier he had found himself wondering whether there was still a challenge in the world worthy of everything he had spent a lifetime learning.

Now a nine-thousand-year-old alien intelligence was floating in front of him asking for help.

The universe, apparently, had a sense of humor.

Then he started laughing again.

"Honestly?"

"Yes."

"At this point breakfast sounds like a fantastic idea."

The orb floated upward.

"Excellent."

Elias looked toward the trail.

Then back at the impossible machine hovering above him.

For the first time in his life, he had the distinct feeling that the project he had spent fifty-five years preparing for had finally arrived.
