Chapter 23: Factory Brainstorming 6

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 23 of 32

← Chapter 22Part A IndexChapter 24 →

The porch became quiet.

Both of them were staring at the whiteboard.

For the first time all day, the problem appeared to have structure.

Not a solution.

A structure.

Which was different.

Much better.

Civilization-scale problems rarely yielded solutions immediately.

But if you could identify the constraints, you could start working.

Elias leaned back in his chair.

"Well."

"Well."

"So we have solved the wife factory problem."

Opi's lights brightened.

"That is still a terrible name."

"It's a fantastic name."

"It is not."

"It absolutely is."

The orb gave up.

Finally Opi spoke again.

"I believe we have made progress."

"We have."

"Significant progress."

"Absolutely."

The orb dimmed slightly.

"Unfortunately."

Elias groaned.

"There is always an unfortunately."

"There is."

The globe expanded.

Now dozens of barriers appeared.

Language.

Distance.

Culture.

Immigration.

Family expectations.

Religion.

Geography.

Legal systems.

Education.

Social norms.

Economic differences.

The list continued growing.

Opi highlighted them one by one.

"We still possess a matching problem."

Elias nodded.

"True."

"It is simply a different matching problem."

"Also true."

"The relative status issue may be reduced."

"Potentially."

"But now we encounter other constraints."

The list continued expanding.

By the time Opi finished, it looked like a military planning document.

Elias stared at it.

Then smiled.

Which confused Opi.

"Why are you smiling?"

"Because this is better."

The orb paused.

"Better?"

"Way better."

He pointed at the board.

"Look at this."

"What am I looking at?"

"Engineering."

The orb became quiet.

Elias continued.

"Status mismatch is hard."

"Yes."

"Changing an entire civilization's values is hard."

"Yes."

"Changing mating preferences is hard."

"Yes."

He pointed at the list.

"Languages?"

"Learnable."

"Culture?"

"Understandable."

"Distance?"

"Solvable."

"Travel?"

"Solvable."

"Communication?"

"Solvable."

The grin widened.

"This is a logistics problem."

The orb brightened.

Interesting.

Elias immediately noticed.

"There it is."

"What?"

"Your tell."

"I do not possess a tell."

"You absolutely possess a tell."

The orb ignored him.

Because he was right.

This really was different.

The husband factory required rebuilding people.

This new problem required rebuilding women and then connecting them to the men.

Entirely different challenge.

Potentially easier.

Potentially harder.

But at least it was concrete.

Elias stood and began pacing again.

"I actually think the husband factory is easier."

"Explain."

"We've already fixed a lot."

"Such as?"

"Health."

"Correct."

"Income."

"Correct."

"Housing."

"Correct."

"Purpose."

"Correct."

"Addiction."

"Correct."

"Hope."

The orb brightened.

"An important variable."

"Exactly."

Elias pointed toward the valley.

"We know how to improve men."

"We've already proven it."

Living evidence.

People who six months earlier had been sleeping under bridges.

Now working.

Building.

Planning.

Living.

"So the larger challenge may be connection."

"Exactly."

Elias sat down again.

"Which means we should focus on barriers."

The globe returned.

Opi highlighted immigration.

"Historically this would be one of the largest barriers."

"Yep."

"Visas."

"Yep."

"Residency."

"Yep."

"Travel permissions."

"Yep."

Then a slow smile spread across Elias's face.

The dangerous smile.

The one Opi had learned to distrust.

"Oh no."

"Oh yes."

"What?"

Elias pointed directly at her.

"My favorite quantum intelligence solves that problem."

The orb paused.

"I am the only quantum intelligence you know."

"That's why you're my favorite."

"I dislike where this conversation is going."

Elias laughed.

"Think about it."

"No."

"Come on."

"No."

"We don't need visas if people aren't crossing borders the normal way."

The orb dimmed suspiciously.

"Elias."

"What?"

"Elias."

"What?"

The orb became completely still.

"You are attempting to turn me into a transportation system."

The grin widened.

"A space RV."

"Elias."

"A spectacular space RV."

"Elias."

"The greatest space RV in human history."

The orb's lights flashed.

"I am not becoming a flying bus."

"A spaceship."

"No."

"An RV spaceship."

"No."

"A giant intercontinental matchmaking space RV."

Absolutely nothing happened.

Then:

"I regret teaching you creativity."

Elias nearly choked laughing.

The orb floated away from him.

A clear sign of disapproval.

"I refuse."

"You haven't even heard the pitch."

"I refuse."

"It has hot tubs."

"No."

"Observation decks."

"No."

"Panoramic views of Earth."

"No."

"A complimentary breakfast buffet."

The orb accelerated away.

"Elias Mercer."

"Yes?"

"You are not turning me into the largest recreational vehicle in human history."

Elias watched her retreat.

Then shouted after her.

"We can put it on the brochure!"

The orb disappeared through the wall.

For several seconds there was silence.

Then her voice echoed from somewhere inside the house.

"I hate this plan."

Elias smiled.

That usually meant she was already running simulations.

And that meant she didn't hate it nearly as much as she claimed.
