Chapter 11: Factory Brainstorming 1

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 11 of 32

The realization bothered Elias all morning.

By lunchtime it was bothering Opi.

Which meant it was serious.

The two of them sat on the porch overlooking the valley.

Elias had a notebook.

Opi had approximately twelve million years of accumulated Velhari demographic research.

Neither was particularly happy.

"So let's start from the beginning," Elias said.

"An excellent strategy."

"The absolute beginning."

He drew a circle on the paper.

"What creates a baby?"

Opi's lights brightened.

"A man and a woman engaging in reproductive activity."

"Right."

He pointed at the notebook.

"Everything else comes after that."

"Agreed."

"A relationship is useful."

"Very."

"But not required."

"Correct."

Elias nodded.

"Okay."

He wrote another note.

"So why do men and women have sex?"

Opi answered immediately.

"Attraction."

"Exactly."

"No civilization has ever escaped that reality."

The orb rotated slowly.

"Many civilizations have attempted to ignore it."

"They all failed."

"Spectacularly."

Elias smiled.

That sounded about right.

"Okay."

He tapped the notebook.

"What makes men attractive to women?"

"What makes women attractive to men?"

"Let's pretend we're aliens."

"We are halfway there."

Elias ignored that.

Opi projected a display.

"Across thousands of human studies and historical populations, men and women display both overlapping and distinct preferences."

The display changed.

"For men, indicators of health, fertility, youth, physical attractiveness, emotional stability, and cooperative behavior repeatedly appear."

"Nothing surprising there."

"No."

The chart shifted.

"For women, indicators of competence, status, intelligence, confidence, resource acquisition, social standing, reliability, ambition, and physical attractiveness repeatedly appear."

Elias nodded.

"Again, not surprising."

"Correct."

"Humans often become upset when research confirms reality."

"That's because reality doesn't care about feelings."

"An excellent summary."

Elias leaned back.

"So attraction matters."

"Yes."

"A lot."

"Enormously."

The orb displayed another graph.

"This is where things become complicated."

Several curves appeared.

Distributions.

Clusters.

Outliers.

"What am I looking at?"

"Mating concentration."

Elias frowned.

"Meaning?"

"The degree to which sexual and romantic opportunities are distributed unevenly."

The graph expanded.

"As societies become wealthier, safer, and more technologically connected, partner selection often becomes increasingly concentrated."

Elias nodded.

"I've seen some of that research."

"Yes."

The orb highlighted the upper end of a curve.

"A relatively small percentage of highly desirable individuals can attract disproportionate attention."

"Both sexes?"

"Both."

"But especially men."

"Correct."

Elias stared at the graph.

"That creates a problem."

"It can."

The graph vanished.

Elias stood and began pacing.

"Let's assume that happens."

"Very well."

"The most attractive men receive enormous amounts of attention."

"Some evidence supports that."

"Those men are also the least likely to settle down."

"Frequently true."

"They have options."

"Many options."

"They aren't trying to create families."

"Not necessarily."

"They're trying not to create families."

"Often."

Elias pointed toward her.

"Exactly."

He sat back down.

"So if that's happening."

"If."

"Then large numbers of women are pursuing men who have little interest in long-term commitment."

Correct.

"And if that occurs often enough."

"Family formation declines."

"Birth rates decline."

"Potentially."

Elias sighed.

"Wonderful."

Opi was silent.

After several moments he asked:

"What about education?"

The orb immediately projected another chart.

This one was much worse.

Fertility rates.

University attainment.

Graduate degrees.

Career participation.

The trend lines were impossible to miss.

"Well that's uncomfortable."

"Demographic data often is."

Elias studied the chart.

"The more educated women become."

"The lower fertility tends to become."

"Almost everywhere."

"Across all societies."

He rubbed his forehead.

"Okay."

"What do we do with that information?"

"I do not know."

That surprised him.

"You don't?"

"No."

The orb dimmed slightly.

"You cannot uneducate people."

"No."

"You should not uneducate people."

"Definitely not."

"Nor is there sufficient time for generational solutions."

Elias nodded.

"Exactly."

He thought about Seoul.

One of the examples Opi had shown him.

An advanced civilization with fertility rates collapsing toward levels that appeared mathematically unsustainable.

"If some societies continue this trend," he said quietly, "they disappear."

"Eventually."

The word lingered.

Eventually.

One of the most terrifying words in demographics.

Because eventually always arrives.

"So what actually matters?"

Elias asked.

"What can we change now?"

Opi became quiet.

For nearly twenty seconds.

A very long time by her standards.

Finally she spoke.

"Perhaps we are asking the wrong question."

Elias looked up.

"What question should we ask?"

The orb projected a single sentence into the air.

WHY DO PEOPLE CHOOSE TO FORM FAMILIES?

Elias stared at it.

"Isn't that the same question?"

"No."

The words vanished.

"Attraction creates opportunities."

"Families require decisions."

The orb floated closer.

"Those are not the same thing."

The statement hit him harder than expected.

Attraction creates opportunities.

Families require decisions.

Opi continued.

"Many people meet attractive partners."

"Few build successful families."

"Those are separate processes."

Elias slowly nodded.

For the first time all afternoon he felt they might be getting somewhere.

"So attraction isn't the whole answer."

"No."

"It's just the doorway."

"Correct."

The orb brightened.

"If we wish to increase births."

"We must understand what happens after attraction."

Elias leaned back in his chair.

For several minutes neither of them spoke.

The valley stretched out below them.

Peaceful.

Quiet.

Unaware that two minds were attempting to solve one of the oldest problems in civilization.

Not how to make people desire one another.

Humans had solved that long ago.

The harder question was why so many people who desired one another, no longer seemed capable of building a future together.

And for the first time, Elias suspected that question might be far more important than homelessness, poverty, politics, or even technology.

Because every civilization ultimately depended on a simple fact.

Someone had to choose to have babies.

And more and more people were choosing not to.
