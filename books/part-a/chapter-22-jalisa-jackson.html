Chapter 22: Jalisa Jackson

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 22 of 32

← Chapter 21Part A IndexChapter 23 →

Jalisa Jackson loved Mondays.

Most people thought that was insane.

Jalisa thought most people had never spent six years working the streets.

Perspective changed things.

She unlocked the front door of the small outreach center and stepped inside.

The building wasn't impressive.

The roof leaked occasionally.

The carpet needed replacing.

The coffee machine made noises that sounded medically concerning.

To Jalisa it looked beautiful.

Because every woman who walked through those doors still had a chance.

That mattered.

The coffee machine sputtered.

Then somehow produced coffee.

A miracle.

Not the biggest miracle Jalisa had witnessed.

But respectable.

She smiled and poured a cup.

The framed photograph hanging above her desk caught her eye.

A younger version of herself stared back.

Thin.

Scared.

Angry.

The woman in the photograph looked twenty years older than she actually was.

Jalisa remembered her well.

She never missed her.

The first client arrived shortly after eight.

Nineteen years old.

New bruises.

Old fears.

Trying very hard to pretend she wasn't terrified.

Jalisa recognized the look immediately.

She had worn it herself once.

The girl sat down.

Silent.

Defensive.

Waiting to be judged.

Jalisa handed her a cup of coffee.

The girl looked suspicious.

"Why are you helping me?"

Jalisa smiled.

"Because somebody helped me."

The girl frowned.

"That's it?"

"That's it."

The answer seemed to disappoint her.

People expected speeches.

Jalisa preferred results.

By noon she had already argued with two city officials.

One nonprofit director.

And a bureaucrat whose job title appeared to consist entirely of acronyms.

A productive morning.

The city loved meetings.

Jalisa loved helping women.

The two activities were only loosely connected.

For years she had watched millions of dollars flow through programs that somehow produced endless reports and very few rescued lives.

Jalisa had developed strong opinions about this.

She shared them frequently.

Often at high volume.

The bureaucrats hated that.

Jalisa considered this evidence she was doing something correctly.

The afternoon brought better news.

One of her former clients stopped by.

Three years sober.

Married.

Working.

Pregnant.

The young woman was practically glowing.

Jalisa hugged her so hard the girl laughed.

"Careful."

"No."

"Jalisa ."

"No."

The girl laughed again.

Jalisa wiped away a tear.

Then another.

She wasn't embarrassed.

Not even a little.

Years earlier she had attended too many funerals.

Now she preferred baby showers.

A significant improvement.

Around sunset the center finally grew quiet.

Jalisa sat alone at her desk.

The city outside continued moving.

Cars.

People.

Noise.

Life.

She folded her hands.

Not because anyone was watching.

Because she wanted to.

"Thank you Jesus"

The words were simple.

They always were.

Thank you for another day.

Thank you for the women who made it out.

Thank you for the ones still trying.

Thank you for the chance to help.

Jalisa wasn't naive.

She remembered exactly who she used to be.

She remembered cold nights.

Bad men.

Bad choices.

The certainty that her life was over.

Then somehow everything changed.

Some people called it recovery.

Some called it luck.

Jalisa called it Jesus.

The distinction mattered a great deal to her.

Without faith she would be dead.

She believed that as firmly as she believed the sun would rise tomorrow.

Maybe more firmly.

The front door opened.

A young woman stepped inside.

Early twenties.

Scared.

Homeless.

Trying desperately not to show it.

Jalisa recognized the look immediately.

Another one.

Good.

The woman hesitated.

Jalisa smiled.

"Welcome."

The woman looked confused.

"Welcome?"

"Yep."

The young woman glanced around.

"To what?"

Jalisa stood and walked toward her.

A grin spread across her face.

"To your second chance."

For a moment the woman simply stared.

Then she started crying.

Jalisa hugged her.

Outside, Seattle continued doing what Seattle did.

Meetings.

Committees.

Studies.

Reports.

Inside the little outreach center, something much more important was happening.

A life was changing.

Jalisa Jackson had learned long ago that saving the world was probably impossible.

Saving one person?

That happened all the time.

And tomorrow she intended to do it again.
