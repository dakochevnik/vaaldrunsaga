Chapter 28: Ronin Taylor

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 28 of 32

← Chapter 27Part A IndexChapter 29 →

Ronin Taylor lasted almost exactly seventeen minutes.

That was the amount of time between receiving a brand-new set of choppers and telling the first person about it.

In Ronin's defense, seventeen minutes was probably some kind of personal record.

He was not a man known for restraint.

The former meth addict stood in front of a convenience store bathroom mirror grinning like a lunatic.

Thirty years.

Thirty years of bad decisions, drugs, poverty, and neglect had reduced his mouth to a collection of broken stumps and missing spaces.

Now he had perfect teeth.

Perfect.

White.

Straight.

Healthy.

Ronin opened his mouth.

Closed it.

Opened it again.

Still there.

Still real.

Still amazing.

He started laughing hysterically

Then he called his brother.

His brother thought he was lying.

Naturally.

Ronin had spent most of his adult life saying shit that wasn't remotely true.

The problem was that this time he was telling the God's honest truth.

Five minutes later his brother received twelve photographs.

Three videos.

One profanity-filled voicemail.

And a live video call featuring Ronin repeatedly opening his mouth like an unusually excited alligator.

An hour later six more people knew.

By evening it was thirty.

By midnight it was hundreds.

Ronin possessed a remarkable talent for communication.

Some people called it networking.

Most people called it never shutting up.

The distinction was largely academic.

He told former friends.

Former dealers.

Former coworkers.

Current coworkers.

Family members.

People he hadn't spoken to in years.

People he barely remembered.

People he met standing in line.

People who probably wished they hadn't met him standing in line.

Everybody got the story.

The reactions varied.

Some people were happy.

Some were skeptical.

Some thought he had finally gone insane.

A few were angry.

Several were jealous.

Most were curious.

Curiosity spread fastest.

Curiosity always did.

Especially when attached to impossible things.

Three days later a woman in Portland heard the story.

Two days after that a journalist heard a modified version.

A week later a criminal organization purchased a recording.

A month later several intelligence agencies had active files.

Most of the information was wrong.

That hardly mattered.

The important part was that people were paying attention.

The internet did what the internet always did.

Some declared the story a hoax.

Others declared it a miracle.

Others declared it proof of aliens.

Or government conspiracies.

Or secret military projects.

Or divine intervention.

Humanity possessed an almost infinite capacity for creative nonsense.

Unfortunately, hidden among the nonsense was the truth.

And the truth was far more valuable than the rumors.

Somewhere in Seattle, a venture capitalist became interested.

In Beijing, a researcher became interested.

In Moscow, a bureaucrat became interested.

In Washington D.C., several people became very interested.

Not because of Ronin.

Nobody cared about Ronin.

They cared about what had happened to him.

The implications.

The possibilities.

The power.

A perfect set of teeth was not particularly important.

Millions of dentists created beautiful smiles every year.

That wasn't the issue.

The issue was that Ronin's teeth had 'supposedly' appeared almost instantly.

No surgery.

No recovery.

No explanation.

No bill.

No technology anyone recognized.

No process anyone understood.

That changed the equation.

Suddenly the question wasn't:

How did he get new teeth?

The question became:

What else can they do?

That question proved far more dangerous.

Two weeks later Ronin sat in a diner eating a steak.

A very large steak.

One requiring extensive chewing.

Mostly because he could.

The man across from him had already heard the story three times.

Ronin was beginning a fourth.

"Then the floating ball says—"

The man raised a hand.

"You told me this already."

"I know."

"So why are you telling me again?"

Ronin grinned.

A huge grin.

A grin featuring every single one of his beautiful new teeth.

"Because look at them."

The man sighed.

Far away, events were already moving.

Questions.

Investigations.

Plans.

Schemes.

Ambitions.

Some people wanted to help.

Some wanted to profit.

Some wanted control.

A few wanted ownership.

The smartest among them all arrived at the same conclusion.

If the stories were true, whoever controlled the miracle would control the future.

None of them yet understood the problem.

The miracle could not be controlled.

And the future had already chosen different owners.

Meanwhile Ronin Taylor ordered dessert.

The world was beginning to change.

Civilizations would shift.

Governments would panic.

Fortunes would be made and destroyed.

Entire industries would eventually disappear.

The first snowflake had already fallen.

The avalanche was inevitable.

Ronin took another bite of cheesecake.

It was considerably easier with actual teeth in his mouth.
