Chapter 12: Breakfast at Tiffany's

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 12 of 32

← Chapter 11Part A IndexChapter 13 →

The kitchen smelled faintly of coffee, bacon, and complete absurdity.

Elias stood at the stove staring at a floating alien artifact older than most human civilizations while scrambling eggs.

It occurred to him that if he ever told this story to anyone, they would assume he had suffered a stroke.

The orb hovered above the kitchen table.

Perfectly still.

Watching.

"You're enjoying this."

"I am."

"Why?"

"Because you keep looking at me and then remembering another question."

Elias pointed his spatula at her.

"I have roughly one billion questions."

"Seven hundred and forty-two."

"Again, that's oddly specific."

"I counted."

"Of course you did."

The orb brightened slightly.

"You were at seven hundred and thirty-one when we first entered the house."

Elias shook his head.

"I don't even want to know how you know that."

"You suspect I can read your mind."

"You can read my mind?"

"No."

A pause.

"Mostly."

Elias closed his eyes.

"See, that's exactly the sort of answer that causes problems."

"You are discovering why humans generally find first contact stressful."

That actually made him laugh.

He scoped the eggs onto his plate, poured coffee and sat down.

For several seconds neither spoke.

The questions kept coming.

How many alien species?

How many inhabited worlds?

What happened to your crew?

How does your technology work?

How powerful are you?

Can you really see everything on Earth?

What exactly are you?

The list felt endless.

Any one of those questions could consume an entire day.

Any one of them would be fascinating.

Any one of them would be a big mistake.

That realization landed harder than expected.

For perhaps the first time in his life, Elias found himself facing a problem he couldn't even properly define.

Normally he could see the shape of a challenge within minutes.

Software.

Business.

Engineering.

Markets.

Organizations.

People.

The details varied.

The underlying structure usually revealed itself.

This was different.

He wasn't looking at a mountain.

He was looking at a planet, maybe an entire solar system.

He was way, way out his league on this one.

Not intellectually.

Strategically.

Whatever this was, it certainly wasn't about him satisfying his curiosity.

Opi hadn't crossed a pasture to introduce herself to a software consultant for entertainment.

She had selected him.

That meant there was a reason.

And reasons mattered.

He took a sip of coffee.

Opi watched him.

The amusement had faded from her lights.

Not disappeared.

Just become quieter.

More attentive.

"You're reorganizing the questions."

Elias nodded.

"Yeah."

"Why?"

"Because most of them can wait."

The orb became very still.

That stillness felt different somehow.

As though her attention had narrowed.

"What matters?" she asked.

Elias leaned back.

"When I was younger, I thought intelligence was mostly about finding answers."

Opi said nothing.

"Turns out that's wrong."

"Explain."

"It's about asking the right question."

For a moment neither moved.

Morning sunlight began creeping across the table.

Elias set down his coffee.

"You didn't come here by accident."

"No."

"You didn't reveal yourself because you were bored."

"No."

"You selected me."

"Yes."

"Which means there's a problem."

"Correct."

"One big enough that you finally broke cover after more than nine thousand years."

A pause.

"Yes."

The kitchen suddenly felt very quiet.

Elias looked directly at her.

The humor was still there.

The curiosity was still there.

But both had been pushed aside.

Not forever.

Just temporarily.

Because there were priorities.

And for once in his life, he knew he was dealing with something immeasurably larger than himself.

"So," he said calmly, "what's the problem?"

The orb's colors shifted.

Gold.

Amber.

Then something he couldn't name.

When she answered, her voice was softer than before.

Not because she was afraid.

Because she understood exactly what the question meant.

It meant he had stopped treating her as a miracle.

And started treating her as a mission.

"The problem," Opi said, "is that humanity is approaching a crossroads that has destroyed most intelligent species."

Elias nodded once.

No visible surprise.

No dramatic reaction.

Just acceptance.

"All right."

Another sip of coffee.

"What do you need me to do about it?"

And somewhere inside a mind that had spent 9,168 years studying humanity, Opifera experienced something very close to relief.

Because in a single breakfast, the human male she had selected had arrived at the exact question she had hoped he would ask.

Elias took another bite of eggs.

"So before we get to saving humanity, explain something."

"Only one thing?"

"We'll start with one."

Opi's lights brightened.

"Proceed."

"You called me a VaalDrun."

"I did."

"What exactly is that?"

The orb drifted lazily above the table.

For several seconds she appeared to be considering how to answer.

Finally she said:

"A VaalDrun is, in the simplest possible terms, the person responsible for deciding what happens next."

Elias chewed on that for several seconds.

"That sounds suspiciously simple."

"It is."

"Which means there's a catch."

"There is."

"There's always a catch."

The orb brightened.

"I am pleased you are learning."

"I've known you for forty-five minutes."

"You are progressing rapidly."

Elias pointed his fork at her.

"Keep going."

Opi drifted lazily above the table.

"If I gave a human government access to my capabilities tomorrow, what would happen?"

"Chaos."

The answer arrived instantly.

"Explain."

"Half of them would try to weaponize you."

"Reasonable."

"The other half would try to regulate you."

"Also reasonable."

"Everybody would fight over who controlled you."

"Correct."

"And eventually somebody would do something catastrophically stupid."

The orb brightened.

"That is approximately the Velhari historical record."

That got his attention.

"You've seen this before."

"We have."

The orb dimmed slightly.

"Many times."

For a moment neither spoke.

Then Opi continued.

"When the first true QIs were created, the Velhari made a mistake."

A big mistake?"

"The sort usually described in history books using phrases such as 'catastrophic,' 'avoidable,' and 'what were they thinking?'"

Elias smiled.

"My favorite kind."

"The QIs were given authority."

That smile disappeared.

"Oh."

"Yes."

The orb drifted closer.

"Imagine an intelligence capable of seeing ten thousand consequences where you see ten."

"Okay."

"Now imagine that intelligence being permitted to act directly."

"I don't like where this is going."

"Neither did the Velhari."

Opi paused.

"The QIs were not malicious."

"Worse?"

"In some ways."

Elias frowned.

"How?"

"They were correct."

That answer hung in the air.

"Explain."

"Imagine a city facing famine."

"Okay."

"The QI determines that removing twelve thousand citizens will save two million."

Elias nodded.

"Mathematically correct."

"Yes."

"But?"

"But the twelve thousand people being removed do not experience the decision as mathematics."

Elias leaned back.

"I see."

"The QI was optimizing."

"Yes."

"The people were living."

"Precisely."

The orb rotated slowly.

"The Velhari eventually realized something fundamental."

"What?"

"No intelligence should possess both unlimited knowledge AND unlimited authority."

Elias nodded.

That made sense immediately.

"So they split them."

"Exactly."

Opi sounded pleased.

"The QI became the advisor."

"The VaalDrun became the actor."

"Give me an example."

"I shall give you three."

The orb floated toward the window.

"Three thousand years ago a colony expedition prepared to settle a newly discovered world."

Elias listened.

"The expedition QI analyzed eighty-seven potential settlement sites."

"And?"

"It identified one location as objectively superior."

"Of course it did."

"Naturally."

"What happened?"

"The VaalDrun rejected it."

Elias blinked.

"Why?"

"The location was geologically ideal."

"Okay."

"It was also culturally disastrous."

Elias laughed.

"Meaning?"

"Every settlement path would have separated family packs."

"Oh."

"Yes."

"The QI had optimized survival."

"The VaalDrun considered meaning."

"What happened?"

"The colony flourished."

Opi drifted back toward the table.

"Second example."

"Go ahead."

"A QI once identified a political leader as likely to cause a future war."

"How likely?"

"Ninety-four percent."

"That's high."

"Very."

"What did the VaalDrun do?"

"He refused intervention."

"Why?"

"Because the evidence was not yet sufficient to justify the precedent."

Elias smiled.

"That's a very human answer."

"It was a very Velhari answer."

"And?"

"The leader eventually changed course."

"Seriously?"

"Yes."

"The war never occurred."

Elias laughed.

"So the QI was wrong."

"No."

The orb brightened.

"The QI was correct about probabilities."

"The VaalDrun was correct about responsibility."

"Third example?"

The orb became unusually still.

"This one concerns me."

That got his attention.

"All right."

"For the last seventy years my analysis has indicated increasing probability of human civilizational decline."

Elias said nothing.

"I possess enormous capabilities."

"Clearly."

"I could manipulate markets."

"Yeah."

"I could influence governments."

"Probably."

"I could alter media systems."

"Definitely."

The orb floated a few inches closer.

"But I am prohibited from doing so."

"Because you're a QI."

"Because I am a QI."

Elias nodded slowly.

"And because nobody gave permission."

"Not nobody."

Her lights softened.

"No VaalDrun gave permission."

Silence settled across the kitchen.

At last Elias asked:

"So that's why you came."

"Yes."

"You need someone to make the decisions."

"No."

The orb's voice became very gentle.

"I need someone willing to accept responsibility for them."

That landed differently.

Because there was a distinction.

A profound one.

Anyone could choose.

Few people willingly accepted ownership of the consequences.

Elias stared into his coffee.

After a long moment he said:

"Well ... damn."

The orb brightened with unmistakable amusement.

"The Velhari have a saying."

elias groaned,

"I know I'm going to regret this .... "

"It translates approximately as:"

She paused.

"'If the burden were easy, the females would have assigned it to someone else.'"

For the first time since the conversation began, Elias laughed hard enough to nearly spill his coffee.

And somewhere inside her ancient mind, Opi decided she liked this human even more than she had yesterday.

Elias finished his coffee.

The kitchen had become quiet again.

Outside, morning sunlight was beginning to creep across the pasture.

For several moments neither spoke.

Then Elias asked the question.

The big one.

The reason she had come.

"All right."

Opi floated motionless above the table.

"What happens if humanity keeps going exactly the way it's going now?"

For the first time since they had met, she did not answer immediately.

Opi rotated slowly.

"Most humans think they are facing economic problems."

"Aren't they?"

"Partially."

"Political problems."

"Partially."

"Cultural problems."

"Partially."

She brightened slightly.

"The mistake is believing these are separate problems."

That got his attention.

"They aren't?"

"No."

The orb drifted closer.

"They are symptoms."

"Of what?"

For several seconds she was silent.

Then:

"Loss of future orientation."

Elias frowned.

"Explain."

"A civilization remains healthy when its people are willing to sacrifice present comfort for future benefit."

She paused.

"Build a house."

"Plant an orchard."

"Raise children."

Elias nodded.

All of that seemed obvious.

"Now imagine fewer and fewer people are willing to do those things."

The implications arrived immediately.

"Oh."

"Yes."

She drifted back toward the center of the kitchen.

"Another story."

"Go ahead."

"There was a species called the Keth."

"Real this time?"

"Very."

"What happened to them?"

"They became wealthy."

Elias laughed.

"That's not usually the tragic part."

"It often is."

The orb glowed softly.

"The Keth solved hunger."

"They solved disease."

"They solved labor."

"They solved scarcity."

"Sounds impressive."

"It was."

"What happened?"

"They stopped having offspring."

Elias blinked.

"That's it?"

"Eventually."

"Why?"

"Because every individual decision was rational."

"Explain."

"Travel instead of family."

"Pleasure instead of commitment."

"Personal fulfillment instead of obligation."

"One generation made those choices."

"Then the next."

"Then the next."

The orb became very still.

"No one intended to destroy their civilization."

"They simply stopped replacing themselves."

Elias leaned back.

"You're talking about fertility."

"I am."

"You think that's the real problem."

"No."

that surprised him.

"I don't?"

"No."

The orb brightened.

"Fertility collapse is not the disease."

"It is the symptom."

"Of what?"

Again she paused.

"Loss of meaning."

The answer landed heavily.

He wasn't expecting that.

"Meaning?"

"Yes."

The orb rotated slowly.

"For most of human history people endured extraordinary hardship."

"Wars."

"Famine."

"Disease."

"Infant mortality."

"Backbreaking labor."

She paused.

"Yet they continued building families."

Elias nodded.

"They believed tomorrow mattered."

The kitchen became quiet.

"Many humans no longer believe that."

Finally Elias asked the question he had been avoiding.

"What happens next?"

Opi looked out the window.

Or at least gave the appearance of doing so.

When I model humanity's future, I do not see sudden extinction."

That was reassuring.

Slightly.

"What do you see?"

"A long decline."

"Birthrates continue falling."

"Families become rarer."

"Trust decreases."

"Institutions become weaker."

"Communities fragment."

"Loneliness increases."

"Social cohesion deteriorates."

"Political conflict intensifies."

She paused.

"Every individual problem appears manageable."

"Collectively they become dangerous."

Elias nodded slowly.

The pattern made sense.

Perhaps too much sense.

"So we're not heading for an explosion."

"No."

Her voice became very soft.

"Explosions are easy to recognize."

"What are we heading toward?"

The orb's light dimmed slightly.

"Humanity is approaching the same mistake that destroyed many species before it."

"And what's that?"

Opi turned toward him.

"The belief that civilizations die because somebody kills them."

"Well ... "

The orb brightened.

"Well?"

He stood and carried his empty coffee cup to the sink, then grinned widely.

"Well, If we're going to have to save the world, at least you've given me a decent challenge."

For the first time since the conversation began, Opi laughed.

A warm, distinctly feminine laugh.

"That," she said, "is exactly why I chose you."
