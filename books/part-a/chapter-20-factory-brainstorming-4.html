Chapter 20: Factory Brainstorming 4

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 20 of 32

← Chapter 19Part A IndexChapter 21 →

For nearly thirty seconds neither of them spoke.

The realization sat between them.

Manufacturing husbands.

Elias started laughing.

Then Opi started laughing.

Which was considerably more alarming.

The orb's lights flickered in a pattern Elias had come to recognize as amusement.

"We accidentally built a husband factory."

"We did."

"A literal husband factory."

"An unintentional husband factory."

Elias pointed toward the valley.

"Look at what we've been doing."

"Housing."

"Health."

"Employment."

"Purpose."

"Community."

"Responsibility."

The list continued.

Opi added:

"Reduced addiction."

"Improved physical fitness."

"Increased social trust."

"Improved emotional stability."

"Reduced criminal behavior."

Elias threw his hands up.

"Those are husband upgrades."

The orb paused.

"That is a disturbingly effective description."

"Thank you."

Elias sat back in his chair.

The more he thought about it, the funnier it became.

For months they had believed they were building a homelessness recovery program.

Instead they had accidentally created a system that transformed broken men into capable ones.

A husband factory.

A civilization-scale husband factory.

The thought was ridiculous.

Which was usually a sign it might be important.

"So now what?" Opi asked.

Elias grinned.

"Simple."

"We manufacture better husbands."

"That sounds like a mission statement."

"It kind of does."

The orb projected a mock advertisement into the air.

PARALLEL INDUSTRIES

PREMIUM HUSBANDS SINCE 2028

Elias nearly fell out of his chair laughing.

"Okay, that's actually funny."

"I know."

For several minutes they continued brainstorming increasingly absurd slogans.

Built To Last.

Now With Emotional Maturity.

Fully Employed And House Trained.

At one point Opi generated a rotating hologram of a man with a quality assurance sticker on his forehead.

Elias laughed so hard he spilled his coffee.

Then suddenly Elias stopped laughing.

The smile faded.

Opi noticed immediately.

"What?"

Elias stared out toward the valley.

"We've got a problem."

The orb became still.

"What problem?"

"The husband factory."

"You were just celebrating the husband factory."

"I was."

"Then why is it a problem?"

Elias pointed toward the whiteboard.

"Because eventually it works."

Opi considered that.

"That is generally considered desirable."

"Not if we haven't thought it through."

The orb waited.

Elias stood and began pacing.

"Let's say ten years from now we've fixed five million men."

"Possible."

"Let's say twenty million."

"Also possible."

"Then what?"

Opi's lights brightened slightly.

"Pair formation."

"With who?"

The orb paused.

That was a valid question.

For several moments neither of them spoke.

Finally Opi answered.

"American women."

Elias immediately shook his head.

"No."

"No?"

"No."

"Explain."

Elias pointed toward the valley.

"The men we're helping aren't competing with average American men."

"True."

"They aren't losing girlfriends to doctors and lawyers."

"Also true."

"They weren't one self-help book away from marriage."

The orb ran through several million case studies.

The conclusion was immediate.

He was correct.

Most of the men entering the Parallel system occupied the bottom tiers of social and economic functioning.

Many had histories of homelessness.

Addiction.

Mental illness.

Trauma.

Criminal records.

Years of instability.

Even after recovery, those labels would often remain.

"Interesting," Opi said.

"Exactly."

Elias nodded.

"We aren't taking husbands away from American women."

"We're creating husbands that otherwise never would have existed."

The orb continued processing.

The effect on existing American pair formation rates would be extremely small.

Perhaps statistically invisible.

These men had largely exited the reproductive marketplace long before Elias ever met them.

The husband factory wasn't reshuffling the deck.

It was adding entirely new cards.

"That is an important distinction."

"It is."

The orb dimmed thoughtfully.

"Then eventually the system requires additional women."

"Yep."

"Large numbers."

"Very large numbers."

For several seconds neither spoke.

Then Opi said:

"We could recruit internationally."

"We could."

The orb brightened.

Then dimmed again.

A flaw had appeared.

"I dislike this."

"Why?"

"Because if we recruit healthy, functional women from other nations, we merely create the same demographic damage somewhere else."

Elias pointed at her.

"Exactly."

"Removing desirable women from foreign populations would reduce pair formation opportunities for local men."

"Exactly."

For nearly a minute they sat in silence.

Then Opi spoke.

"What if we use the same solution?"

Elias frowned.

"The same solution?"

"The one we used here."

He stared at her.

Then slowly smiled.

A dangerous smile.

The sort of smile that usually meant civilization was about to get redesigned again.

"Oh."

The orb brightened.

"Oh."

The realization struck both of them simultaneously.

The husband factory.

The answer had been sitting directly in front of them.

Elias laughed.

"You know, sometimes we're idiots."

"Frequently."

"We didn't recruit healthy American men."

"We repaired damaged American men."

"Exactly."

The orb's lights grew brighter.

Across her simulations entire populations suddenly appeared.

Women trapped by addiction.

Women suffering from severe trauma.

Women with untreated mental illness.

Women living in extreme poverty.

Women carrying physical disabilities that could be corrected.

Women abandoned by failing social systems.

Women who had effectively been written off as marriageable wife material by their own societies.

Not bad people.

Not inferior people.

Broken people.

The same category that had once described many of the men now living in the Parallel.

"There are tens of millions of them."

"There have to be."

"Hundreds of millions, potentially."

Elias nodded.

"And unlike traditional immigration programs, we're not taking functioning people away from anybody."

"We are restoring people who were already excluded."

The orb's processors accelerated.

The implications expanded rapidly.

Many of those women might choose America.

Many would not.

Many would choose to remain in their own nations.

Which produced another realization.

A much larger one.

"Elias."

"What?"

"This was never going to be just an American project."

He smiled.

"I know."

The Parallel was already spreading beyond borders.

Eventually there would be Parallel communities in dozens of nations.

Then hundreds.

Some of the women they restored would become citizens of those future communities.

Teachers.

Leaders.

Builders.

Entrepreneurs.

Mothers.

And perhaps most importantly—

Ambassadors.

For several seconds neither of them spoke.

The implications continued expanding.

Across hundreds of nations.

Thousands of future communities.

Millions of restored lives.

Eventually Elias leaned back in his chair.

"You know what the funny part is?"

"What?"

"We spent half an hour worrying about disrupting dating markets."

"We took homeless men. Addicts. Men with criminal records. Men most of society had already written off."

"Accurate."

"How many American women were fighting over those guys?"

Opi went silent.

Then her lights brightened.

"Statistically, none."

"Exactly."

The orb reviewed data on all 780,000 homeless in the USA.

The conclusion was obvious.

Most of the men they recovered had already exited the mating market years earlier.

"We didn't take husbands from anyone," Elias said.

"We created new, bonus husbands."

The distinction mattered.

The husband factory wasn't reshuffling the deck.

It was adding new cards.

Elias pointed at her.

"Now look at the wife factory."

Opi immediately understood.

Women trapped by addiction.

Trauma.

Mental illness.

Poverty.

Physical disabilities.

Women abandoned by failing systems and largely excluded from family formation.

The same problem.

The same solution.

"We would not be taking wives from local men."

"No."

"We would be restoring women who had already fallen out of the system."

For several moments Opi simply processed.

Then her lights brightened.

"In both cases, the effect on local mating dynamics will be negligible."

"Exactly."

Neither factory was recruiting from the center of society.

Both were recovering people from the margins.

People who had effectively been removed from pair formation already.

The system wasn't consuming human capital.

It was recovering it.

Millions of restored men.

Millions of restored women.

Millions of families that otherwise never would have existed.

Finally Elias smiled.

"So now we have a husband factory."

"Yes."

"And a wife factory."

"Yes."

"And neither one really competes with anybody."

Elias raised his coffee mug.

"To husband factories."

The orb brightened.

"To wife factories."
