Chapter 19: Gloria Martinez

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 19 of 32

← Chapter 18Part A IndexChapter 20 →

Gloria Martinez hated mornings.

Mostly because she kept waking up in them.

The joke wasn't particularly funny anymore.

Neither was diabetes.

She sat on the edge of a cot inside a crowded shelter and stared at her feet.

Her left foot.

Especially her left foot.

The doctors had been talking about it again.

Not directly.

Doctors almost never spoke directly.

They used words like "complications" and "management" and "quality of life."

Gloria preferred honesty.

The foot was dying.

She knew it.

They knew it.

Everyone knew it.

Nobody wanted to say it.

At sixty-two years old, Gloria had become an expert at recognizing bad news wrapped in polite language.

The shelter around her slowly came alive.

People coughing.

People moving.

People arguing.

People trying to survive another day.

Gloria stood carefully.

Her foot screamed.

"Good morning to you too."

The foot remained pessimistic.

She limped toward the bathroom.

The woman staring back from the mirror barely looked familiar.

Gray hair.

Tired eyes.

Skin hanging a little looser every year.

The face of someone who had spent too much time losing.

She remembered another woman.

A younger one.

Married.

Working.

Healthy.

Laughing.

That woman had plans.

This woman had insulin.

Sometimes.

When she could get it.

When she could afford it.

When somebody didn't steal it.

Progress.

The bus arrived late.

Again.

The driver didn't look at her.

Gloria didn't blame him.

Most people avoided eye contact with homeless people.

They were afraid homelessness might be contagious.

She found the whole thing amusing.

Not funny.

Just absurd.

One bad divorce.

One medical problem.

One layoff.

One stretch of bad luck.

Most people were a lot closer to her situation than they realized.

They just hadn't done the math yet.

The food bank line stretched around the block.

Gloria took her place near the back.

A cold wind cut through her jacket.

She ignored it.

A man farther ahead was talking about sports.

Someone else was complaining about politics.

Someone behind her smelled strongly of alcohol.

Life continued.

Even here.

Especially here.

That was the strange thing.

Nobody gave up as easily as outsiders imagined.

People kept trying.

Even when trying stopped making sense.

By late afternoon her foot hurt badly enough that she sat on a concrete planter outside a strip mall.

Traffic moved past.

People carried shopping bags.

Families came and went.

Normal life.

The kind she used to have.

For a few minutes she watched a little girl holding her father's hand.

The child couldn't have been more than six.

She was talking nonstop.

The father listened patiently.

Gloria smiled.

Then looked away.

The smile hurt more than her foot.

There had been a time she thought she would have grandchildren.

Funny how life worked.

You made plans.

Life laughed.

That evening she checked her blood sugar.

The number was terrible.

Not surprising.

Just terrible.

She stared at the meter for a while.

Then put it away.

What exactly was she supposed to do about it?

She was already doing her best.

The problem with being poor was that eventually people started offering solutions that cost money.

As if nobody had thought of that.

As if she hadn't spent years wishing she could afford her own advice.

The sun had disappeared when Gloria finally settled onto a bench near the shelter.

Her foot throbbed.

Her back hurt.

Her hands hurt.

Everything hurt.

She closed her eyes.

Tomorrow would probably be the same.

Another line.

Another bus.

Another day.

Another reminder that the world had moved on without her.

The thought should have made her angry.

Mostly it just made her tired.

So very tired.

A cold breeze drifted across the parking lot.

Gloria pulled her jacket tighter.

Then she looked up at the stars.

A habit from childhood.

Her father had loved astronomy.

He used to point out constellations from their backyard.

Back when life had seemed endless.

Back when she believed good things were always waiting somewhere ahead.

She smiled at the memory.

For a moment she almost felt young again.

Then her foot reminded her otherwise.

"Yeah, yeah."

The stars offered no opinion.

Gloria sighed.

Tomorrow would come.

Whether she wanted it to or not.
