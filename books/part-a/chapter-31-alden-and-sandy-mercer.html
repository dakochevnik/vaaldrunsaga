Chapter 31: Alden & Sandy Mercer

Book 1: The Parallel — Part A

Chapter 31 of 32

← Chapter 30Part A IndexChapter 32 →

The actual process of creating the new Alden Mercer proved disappointingly anti-climactic.

Once the date had been chosen, they packed up everything they wanted to keep.

Furniture.

Clothes.

Photographs.

Kitchen supplies.

Books.

Tools.

Memories.

An entire life reduced to a collection of objects piled into a Southern California living room.

Then Opi simply moved all of it.

People included.

One moment they were south of LAX.

The next they were standing in Stevens County.

The cabin sat near the edge of one of the meadows on Elias's property.

Small.

Comfortable.

Private.

Built almost entirely to Sandy's specifications.

Opi had politely ignored several suggestions from Elias.

"This wall should go here."

"No."

"Why?"

"Because Sandy is correct."

"I haven't even finished."

"You were going to suggest something stupid."

Elias had eventually given up.

The result was considerably nicer than anything he would have designed.

He suspected Opi enjoyed that.

Almost as soon as they finished settling in, Alden turned toward them.

"Can we do this right now?"

There was no hesitation in his voice.

No fear.

No uncertainty.

Just eagerness.

The kind of eagerness usually associated with children on Christmas morning.

Elias looked at Opi.

Opi looked at Elias.

Then both looked at Sandy.

She smiled.

"We're ready."

Alden sat in the center of the living room.

His wheelchair beneath him.

The chair that had carried him through almost his entire life.

The chair he would never need again.

For a few moments nobody spoke.

Then Alden looked up at Opi.

"One question."

"Yes?"

"What am I going to look like?"

The orb brightened.

"A reasonable question."

"Thank you."

Opi became silent for several seconds.

Processing.

Comparing.

Modeling.

Finally she answered.

"Based upon the available genetic information from your father, I estimate approximately six feet tall."

Alden laughed.

"Six feet?"

"Approximately."

"You're kidding."

"I do not generally joke about anthropometric projections."

Elias sighed.

"Translation?"

"About six feet."

"Thank you."

For a moment Alden became quiet.

Thinking.

Remembering.

His father had been a tall man for a Mercer.

Tough looking. With a deep voice that seemed to operate in slow motion.

It took his father twice as long to finish a sentence as a normal man.

Alden had a very rough childhood - one of the roughest Elias had ever seen.

Father - massive PTSD from 2 tours in Viet Nam as a radio operator in an attack helicopter squadron.

When the internet came into wide use Alden had tracked down some of the men who had served with his father wanting to know more - they told him a story about how his father had fallen for the girl who cleaned their barracks each day - a young woman named Trần Ngọc Anh. One day at noon she had come into the barracks with bombs strapped to her torso by the VC and the other soldiers had to kill her.

After that story the old veteran told Alden that there was a lot more that was even worse and he was sure that Alden's father would never want him to know about any of it.

Alden gave up trying to find out more about his dad after that.

The rest of Alden's life wasn't exactly an improvement - both Aldens father and mother had died in an automobile crash when he was 12 years old leaving him and 4 other siblings as orphans. Elias was then just 22 years old, a poor broke college student, with no job no house and no car, yet he seriously considered taking them all in, very religious foster family had stepped up and taken them all in. It all worked out in the end (mostly).

One more disaster in a life that already seemed to attract them.

Of all the people Elias Mercer had ever met in his long life, his own cousin was both the most unlucky and at the same time, the most positive person he had ever met. He was far more extraordinary than anyone Elias had ever met. And Elias was convinced that nobody on the entire planet deserved what was going to happen next more than Alden.

Alden looked around the room.

At Sandy.

At Elias.

At Opi.

Then finally down at the wheelchair.

His wheelchair.

The thing he hated.

The thing he loved.

The thing that had become part of him.

The thing he was about to lose forever.

His hand rested briefly on the armrest.

Then he smiled.

"Okay."

His voice was steady.

"I'm ready."

Opi's light shifted.

White.

Brilliant.

For less than a second.

Less.

Far less.

Then it was over.

No pain.

No sound.

No drama.

No transformation sequence.

No cosmic energy.

One instant.

That was all.

One instant between two versions of reality.

The wheelchair collapsed sideways.

Empty.

Because Alden Mercer no longer fit inside it.

For a moment nobody moved.

Nobody spoke.

Nobody breathed.

Where Alden Mercer had been sitting now stood a man a little over six feet tall.

narrow shouldered.

Straight backed.

Two arms.

Two legs.

No titanium rods.

No twisted spine.

No wheelchair.

Nothing broken.

Nothing missing.

Nothing damaged.

The room became completely silent.

Even Opi.

Which was rare.

Alden looked down.

Then at his hands.

Then at his feet.

Then back at his hands.

The sequence repeated several times.

As though his brain was refusing to update.

Finally he flexed his fingers.

All ten of them.

Then laughed.

A single startled laugh.

The kind that escaped before a person could stop it.

"Oh my God."

His voice sounded different.

Stronger.

Deeper. Almost as deep as his fathers had been - and he talked much slower too.

Everything sounded different.

Then he took a step.

A real step.

Not a wheelchair.

Not a transfer.

Not a carefully planned maneuver.

Just a step.

Then another.

Then another.

The movement looked awkward.

Almost ridiculous.

Like a giraffe learning to ice skate.

But he was walking.

Actually walking.

For the first time in his life.

Sandy still hadn't moved.

She simply stared.

Her hands covered her mouth.

Tears streamed down her face.

But she wasn't crying.

Not exactly.

She appeared too shocked to cry properly.

Alden finally turned toward her.

The smile immediately vanished.

Because suddenly this became real.

Really real.

"Sandy?"

Her voice emerged several seconds later.

"My God."

Alden swallowed.

"Sandy?"

She slowly walked toward him.

Once.

Twice.

Three steps.

Then stopped directly in front of him.

And then his six foot tall wife looked up.

Actually up.

Not down.

Up.

The realization hit both of them simultaneously.

For forty-seven years Sandy had looked down at her husband.

Now she wasn't.

Now she was looking slightly upward.

Not much.

An inch.

Maybe two.

But enough.

Definitely enough.

The silence stretched.

Finally Sandy narrowed her eyes.

Studied him carefully.

Very carefully.

Then walked around him.

Once.

Twice.

A complete circle.

Like she was inspecting livestock at a county fair.

Alden looked at Elias.

"Elias."

"Yeah?"

"Help."

"No."

Sandy completed another lap.

Then another.

Finally she stopped directly in front of him.

The room held its breath.

Alden held his breath.

Elias held his breath.

Even Opi appeared interested.

Sandy reached out.

Touched his face.

Then nodded.

"Huh."

Alden blinked.

"Huh?"

"Yep."

Another nod.

"Huh."

The confusion became painful.

"What does that mean?"

Sandy smiled.

A slow smile.

Dangerous.

Married.

Experienced.

The kind of smile wives developed after years of practice.

"It means you still have the same ugly face."

The room exploded.

Elias doubled over laughing.

Opi immediately began laughing as well.

Alden stared.

Offended.

Genuinely offended.

"Sandy!"

"What?"

"You could have said handsome."

"I have known you too long for that."

More laughter.

Sandy reached up.

Placed both hands on his cheeks.

Studied him again.

"Still got the giant head too."

Alden pointed accusingly at Elias.

"This is your fault."

"It absolutely is."

"I don't know how I'm going to get even with you but I swear I'll figure something out."

Even Sandy laughed at that.

Then she wrapped her arms around him.

Tightly.

Very tightly.

For a long moment neither spoke.

No jokes.

No laughter.

No teasing.

Just silence.

The good kind.

The kind shared by people who had survived a very long road together.

Eventually Sandy leaned back slightly.

Her eyes were still wet.

"So."

Alden smiled.

"So."

"You realize we're going to have to buy all new clothes."

The smile widened.

"Yeah."

"All of them."

"Yeah."

She looked him up and down again.

Then grinned.

"And for the first time in our marriage, I'm stealing your shirts."

That broke whatever restraint remained.

Everybody laughed again.

Including Alden.

Especially Alden.

Because for the first time in his life, he had absolutely no idea what tomorrow looked like.

As the day wore down Alden got tired of experimenting and headed for the new house for dinner.

Sandy spent most of the day organizing their new home while Alden spent most of his learning how to operate an entirely new body.

The process proved less graceful than he had imagined.

At one point he attempted to jump off the front porch.

He landed directly on his ass.

To be fair, Alden had spent forty-seven years watching other people perform activities that required legs. Actually doing them turned out to be a different matter entirely.

Opi had warned him about that beforehand.

"Your nervous system will adapt quickly," she had explained.

Quickly, it turned out, was not the same thing as instantly.

By late afternoon Alden was sore, frustrated, and exhausted.

Which was impressive considering he was now probably the healthiest human being in Stevens County.

After dinner he and Sandy settled onto the couch and watched a movie.

Alden paid almost no attention to it.

As the evening wore on he became increasingly nervous.

He was about to spend his first night as a completely different man.

With the woman he loved.

What he didn't know was that Sandy was nervous too.

Days earlier, during the planning stages, she had managed to corner Opi privately.

"I need to ask you something."

"You may."

Sandy hesitated.

"What happens if he gets this amazing new body and suddenly realizes he can do better than me?"

Opi had remained silent for several seconds.

Not because she was surprised.

Quite the opposite.

She had predicted the conversation long before Sandy herself knew she was going to have it.

"There are several things I can do," Opi had finally replied.

When Opi finished, Sandy had been delighted with the results.

She wasn't transformed into a movie star.

That wasn't the point.

The years simply seemed lighter.

The stiffness in her joints disappeared.

Old aches vanished.

Her muscles felt strong again.

For the first time in years she felt thirty instead of fifty.

And that had done wonders for her confidence.

Later that evening Alden walked into the bedroom and immediately encountered a new problem.

He had exactly one set of clothes.

None of them were pajamas.

He considered the issue for several seconds.

Then he glanced toward the bed.

Sandy was watching him with extraordinary interest.

"The hell with it," Alden muttered.

He started undressing.

Sandy's eyes grew wider.

Then wider still.

Finally she placed one hand dramatically against her chest and, in what she imagined was a perfect 1940s Hollywood starlet voice, announced:

"Ohhhhh, Big Al, honey..."

She paused for effect.

"It appears more than a few things got upgraded."

Alden turned crimson.

But at the same time every lingering doubt he'd carried for days simply evaporated.

They were going to be fine.

More than fine.

Unfortunately for Alden, at that exact moment Elias happened to be walking behind the cabin on his way back to the house.

The bedroom window was open.

Sandy's performance carried remarkably well.

Elias heard every word.

He laughed so hard he nearly fell over.

Big Al would endure the consequences for the better part of the next week.

Elias considered it one of the finest moments of his life.
